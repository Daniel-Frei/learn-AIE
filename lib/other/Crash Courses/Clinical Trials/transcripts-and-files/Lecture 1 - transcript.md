# Lecture 1 Transcript — Why Clinical Trials Exist

Welcome everyone. This is the first lecture in our crash course on clinical trials. The goal of this course is not to turn you into a biostatistician, a clinical trial manager, or a regulatory affairs specialist in five hours. That would be impossible. The goal is more realistic but still ambitious: by the end of these five lectures, you should be able to have intelligent conversations with people who work in clinical research, pharma, biotech, hospitals, medical device companies, or CROs. You should understand the basic language, the core concepts, the logic behind clinical trials, and the kinds of problems that people in this industry deal with every day.

Today’s lecture is called **Why Clinical Trials Exist**.

The theme is simple:

**Medicine is difficult because humans are noisy systems.**

That sentence is going to guide almost everything we discuss today. Humans are not like clean laboratory systems. We are biologically complex, psychologically complex, socially complex, and medically heterogeneous. We differ in age, genetics, diet, environment, previous disease history, lifestyle, expectations, access to care, and adherence to treatment. On top of that, diseases themselves fluctuate. Symptoms come and go. Some conditions improve naturally. Some worsen unpredictably. Some appear to respond to treatment, but only because the timing was lucky.

So the basic problem in medicine is this:

A patient receives a treatment and then improves.

Did the treatment cause the improvement?

That sounds like a simple question, but it is one of the hardest questions in medicine.

Clinical trials exist because casual observation is not enough to answer it reliably.

By the end of this lecture, you should be able to explain why clinical trials are necessary, distinguish correlation from causation, identify major sources of bias in medical research, understand why randomization became so important, describe the hierarchy of clinical evidence, and explain the basic roles of sponsors, investigators, CROs, regulators, ethics committees, and patients.

Let us begin with the most basic question.

How do we know whether a treatment actually works?

Imagine that someone tells you: “This new treatment works. We gave it to patients, and most of them improved.”

At first, that sounds persuasive. If patients were sick, they got treated, and then they improved, it seems natural to conclude that the treatment worked. This is how humans reason in everyday life. If I press a button and the light turns on, I infer that pressing the button caused the light to turn on. If I take pain medication and my headache improves, I infer that the medication helped.

Often that kind of reasoning is fine. But in medicine, it can be deeply misleading.

Let us use a simple example.

Suppose 100 people develop headaches. A doctor gives all 100 people a new herbal pill. After three days, 80 of them feel better.

Did the pill work?

Many people would instinctively say yes. Eighty percent improved. That sounds impressive.

But now we have to slow down.

What else could explain the improvement?

Headaches often resolve naturally. Some people would have improved without doing anything. Some people may have improved because they expected the pill to help. That is a placebo effect. Some may have started the treatment when the headache was at its worst, and the headache would probably have become less severe anyway. That is regression to the mean. Some may have changed other behaviors at the same time: they slept more, drank more water, avoided alcohol, or stopped staring at a screen. Some may have misremembered how bad the headache was before treatment. Some may simply have experienced random fluctuation.

So the key point is this:

**Improvement after treatment does not imply improvement because of treatment.**

This is one of the most important principles in all of clinical research.

In everyday life, we are used to interpreting sequences as causal. First A happened, then B happened, therefore A caused B. But in medicine, that logic is dangerous. A patient can improve after treatment even if the treatment had no effect. A patient can worsen after treatment even if the treatment was beneficial. A treatment can look helpful in one group of patients and harmful in another. And a treatment can appear effective in observational data but fail when tested rigorously.

This is why medicine needed clinical trials.

For most of medical history, medical practice was not based on the kind of evidence we would expect today. It was based on authority, tradition, biological theories, expert experience, and anecdotal observation. A senior physician believed something worked. A medical school taught it. A hospital adopted it. Patients were treated accordingly. Sometimes the treatment helped. Sometimes it did nothing. Sometimes it harmed people.

A classic historical example is bloodletting. For centuries, bloodletting was used to treat a wide variety of conditions. The logic came from older theories of bodily humors and balance. If a person was ill, perhaps the body had too much blood or an imbalance of fluids, so removing blood might restore balance. To modern ears this sounds strange, but for a long time it seemed biologically plausible within the dominant medical theory of the time. It was also supported by anecdotal experience. A doctor might bleed a patient, the patient might temporarily feel calmer or different, and this could be interpreted as improvement. But in many cases, bloodletting weakened patients and probably worsened outcomes.

The broader lesson is not just that old doctors were wrong. The deeper lesson is that **biological plausibility is not enough**.

A treatment can make sense mechanistically and still fail in humans.

That is still true today.

Modern medicine has many examples where a treatment looked promising based on biology, laboratory studies, early observational evidence, or small uncontrolled studies, but later failed in randomized trials.

One famous example is hormone replacement therapy in postmenopausal women. For years, observational studies suggested that women taking hormone replacement therapy had lower rates of cardiovascular disease. This seemed plausible. Estrogen had biological effects that might protect the cardiovascular system. Many clinicians believed hormone therapy could reduce heart disease risk. But later randomized trials found that the picture was much more complicated and that hormone therapy could increase certain risks. The observational evidence had been affected by confounding. Women who used hormone therapy were often different from women who did not. They tended to have different socioeconomic status, different health behaviors, different access to healthcare, and different baseline risk profiles.

So even in modern medicine, with modern scientific language and plausible mechanisms, we can still be fooled.

This leads to a central idea:

Clinical trials are systematic tools for separating true treatment effects from bias, random variation, placebo effects, natural disease progression, and confounding.

That is the purpose of clinical trials. They are not bureaucratic rituals. They are not merely regulatory hurdles. They exist because the human mind, clinical intuition, and uncontrolled observation are not reliable enough.

Now let us move to the second major part of today’s lecture: why human intuition fails.

Humans are pattern-detection machines. This is one of our greatest strengths. We notice regularities. We infer causes. We generalize from experience. A clinician sees thousands of patients and develops intuition. That intuition can be valuable. Experienced clinicians often notice things that inexperienced people miss.

But the same pattern-detection ability also creates problems.

We see patterns where none exist. We remember dramatic cases more easily than ordinary cases. We notice the patient who improved after an unusual treatment, but forget the many patients who would have improved anyway. We overestimate the importance of recent experiences. We mistake correlation for causation. And once we believe a treatment works, we interpret future cases through that belief.

Imagine a physician gives Drug A to 10 patients. Eight improve. The physician concludes, “Drug A works.”

But what is missing?

The physician never observed what would have happened to those same patients without Drug A. Maybe eight would have improved anyway. Maybe nine would have improved without treatment. Maybe only three would have improved. We do not know.

This is the basic missing comparison.

In clinical research, we are always asking a counterfactual question:

**What would have happened to this same patient if they had not received the treatment?**

That question is impossible to answer directly, because the same patient cannot simultaneously receive and not receive the treatment under identical conditions. Once the patient has taken the drug, we cannot observe the alternative world in which the same patient did not take the drug.

This is sometimes called the fundamental problem of causal inference.

Causal inference is hard because we cannot observe both potential outcomes for the same person at the same time.

Clinical trials solve this problem imperfectly but powerfully by using comparison groups. We cannot observe the same patient in both worlds, but we can compare groups of patients that are similar enough that one group approximates what would have happened to the other group without treatment. Randomization is the strongest tool we have for making those groups comparable.

We will discuss randomization in detail in Lecture 2. For now, the key point is that randomization exists because we need a credible comparison.

Without a comparison group, treatment effects are extremely difficult to interpret.

Let us talk about correlation and causation.

Correlation means that two variables move together. Causation means that one variable directly influences another. In medicine, correlated variables are everywhere. People who take a certain medication may have higher mortality. Does the medication increase mortality? Maybe. But maybe sicker patients are more likely to receive that medication. People who visit doctors more often may be diagnosed with more diseases. Does visiting doctors cause disease? No. People who visit doctors more often may already have symptoms or risk factors. People who take vitamins may appear healthier. Do vitamins cause better health? Maybe in some cases, but vitamin users may also exercise more, smoke less, eat better, and have higher income.

This is confounding.

A confounder is a variable that is associated with both the exposure and the outcome, and can create a misleading association.

Let us use a simple example.

Suppose we observe that people who drink a lot of coffee have higher rates of lung cancer. We might ask: does coffee cause lung cancer? But then we discover that, in this population, heavy coffee drinkers are also more likely to smoke. Smoking causes lung cancer, and smoking is associated with coffee drinking. So smoking confounds the relationship between coffee and lung cancer.

The association between coffee and lung cancer might not be causal at all. It might be explained by smoking.

In clinical medicine, confounding can be subtle and difficult to eliminate. It is not only about obvious variables like age or smoking. It can involve disease severity, physician decision-making, socioeconomic status, adherence, frailty, genetics, health literacy, and access to care.

This is why observational studies can be informative but also dangerous when interpreted causally.

Now, observational studies are not bad. That is important. We do not want a simplistic message that only randomized trials matter and everything else is useless. Observational research is essential in medicine. It can identify safety signals, study rare harms, examine real-world populations, generate hypotheses, and answer questions where randomized trials are impossible or unethical.

But observational studies are more vulnerable to confounding than randomized trials.

That is why clinical trials became central to evidence-based medicine.

Let us now examine the main sources of bias and error that clinical trials are designed to address.

The first is the placebo effect.

A placebo effect occurs when a patient improves because of expectations, context, attention, or belief rather than because of the specific biological action of the treatment.

A patient receives a sugar pill but believes it is a powerful pain medication. Their pain improves. The improvement may be real. Placebo effects are not simply fake or imaginary. Symptoms like pain, nausea, anxiety, fatigue, and subjective wellbeing can genuinely change in response to expectations and context.

This matters because if we give patients a treatment and they improve, some of that improvement may come from the therapeutic ritual itself: seeing a clinician, receiving attention, believing one is being treated, expecting improvement.

So if we want to know whether the treatment itself has a specific effect, we need a comparison. In many drug trials, that comparison is a placebo group. One group receives the active drug. Another group receives an inactive pill that looks the same. Ideally, neither the patients nor the clinicians know who received which. That way, expectations are balanced across groups.

The second source of error is the natural history of disease.

Many diseases improve without intervention. Viral infections often resolve naturally. Many episodes of back pain improve over time. Headaches often go away. Mild depression can fluctuate. Skin conditions can flare and calm down. Symptoms of chronic conditions often vary from week to week.

If patients start treatment during a bad period and later improve, it may look like the treatment worked. But perhaps the disease would have improved anyway.

This is especially important in conditions with fluctuating symptoms. If you only observe before and after treatment, you may mistake natural improvement for treatment effect.

The third source of error is regression to the mean.

Regression to the mean is one of those concepts that sounds statistical but is actually very intuitive once you see it.

Imagine a patient has unusually high blood pressure at a clinic visit. Because the value is extreme, part of it may reflect their true blood pressure, but part may reflect temporary factors: stress, caffeine, poor sleep, measurement error, or random fluctuation. If you measure them again later, the value is likely to be closer to their usual average, even if no treatment was given.

Now imagine a study recruits patients only when their symptoms are severe. This happens often. People seek care when symptoms are bad. Trials enroll patients who meet severity thresholds. But because symptoms fluctuate, many patients will naturally look better at the next measurement. That improvement can be falsely attributed to treatment.

That is regression to the mean.

It is a major reason uncontrolled before-after studies can be misleading.

The fourth source of error is selection bias.

Selection bias occurs when the people included in a study differ systematically from the people we want to make conclusions about, or when treatment groups differ systematically from each other.

For example, patients who volunteer for clinical trials may not represent the average patient. They may be more motivated, more health-conscious, more adherent, or have better access to healthcare. On the other hand, some trials recruit patients with more severe disease because those patients are actively seeking new options. Either way, the study population may differ from the real-world population.

Selection bias can also occur when physicians choose who gets a treatment. Suppose a new treatment is offered preferentially to younger, healthier patients. Those patients may have better outcomes regardless of the treatment. If we compare them to older, sicker patients who did not receive the treatment, the treatment may look effective even if it is not.

The fifth source is observer bias.

Observer bias occurs when researchers or clinicians unconsciously assess outcomes differently depending on what they expect.

Imagine a trial of a new drug for joint pain. If the physician knows which patients received the experimental drug, they may unconsciously interpret ambiguous symptoms more favorably. They may ask questions differently. They may record improvement differently. They may give more encouragement to patients in the treatment group.

This is not necessarily fraud. It is human bias.

Blinding helps reduce observer bias. If the physician does not know which patient received the active drug, their expectations are less likely to influence assessment.

The sixth source is reporting and publication bias.

Publication bias occurs when studies with positive results are more likely to be published than studies with negative or inconclusive results.

Imagine ten small studies test a supplement. By chance, two show positive results and eight show no effect. If only the two positive studies are published, the literature will misleadingly suggest that the supplement works. A doctor reviewing the published literature may never see the missing negative studies.

This is one reason trial registration became important. Researchers are increasingly expected to register trials before they are conducted, making it harder to hide unfavorable results.

Publication bias becomes especially important when we discuss systematic reviews and meta-analysis, because a meta-analysis can only combine the studies that are available. If the available studies are a biased subset of all studies that were actually conducted, the meta-analysis can still be misleading.

Now let us pause and do a short thought exercise.

Suppose a physician develops a new treatment and reports that 80% of patients improved after receiving it.

What alternative explanations could exist besides true treatment efficacy?

You should now be able to name several.

Maybe the disease improves naturally. Maybe patients enrolled when symptoms were unusually severe and then regressed toward their average. Maybe there was a placebo effect. Maybe the patients selected for the treatment were healthier or more motivated. Maybe the outcome was assessed subjectively by someone who believed in the treatment. Maybe only successful cases were reported. Maybe other treatments were used at the same time. Maybe the sample was small and the result was random.

This is the mindset of clinical research.

Clinical research is not cynical. It does not assume every treatment is ineffective. Rather, it is disciplined. It asks: what else could explain this result?

That brings us to the evidence hierarchy.

In medicine, not all evidence is equally reliable. Different types of evidence answer different kinds of questions and have different vulnerabilities.

At the bottom of the traditional hierarchy is expert opinion.

Expert opinion matters. Experienced clinicians and scientists know a lot. They can interpret complex cases, generate hypotheses, and integrate evidence. But expert opinion is vulnerable to bias. Experts can be wrong. They can overgeneralize from experience. They can be influenced by tradition, incentives, professional identity, and memorable cases.

Above expert opinion, we often place case reports.

A case report describes one patient. For example, a clinician gives a drug and observes an unexpected severe reaction. Or a patient with a rare disease responds dramatically to a treatment. Case reports are useful for detecting unusual events, rare diseases, and new hypotheses. But they are weak for establishing causation. One patient improving after treatment does not prove that the treatment caused the improvement.

A case series describes multiple patients. For example, a hospital reports outcomes in 20 patients treated with a new surgical technique. This is more informative than a single case, but it still usually lacks a control group. Without a comparison group, it is difficult to know what would have happened otherwise.

Next are observational studies.

Observational studies observe patients in real-world or routine settings without randomly assigning treatment. Common types include cohort studies and case-control studies.

In a cohort study, researchers follow groups of people over time and compare outcomes. For example, they might compare patients taking Drug A with patients not taking Drug A and examine rates of hospitalization.

In a case-control study, researchers start with people who have an outcome, such as a rare cancer, and compare them with people who do not, looking backward to identify exposures.

Observational studies can be powerful. They are often faster and cheaper than randomized trials. They can include large populations. They can study rare harms and long-term outcomes. They can be more representative of real-world practice. But they are vulnerable to confounding and selection bias.

Above observational studies are randomized controlled trials, or RCTs.

An RCT randomly assigns participants to treatment or control. The purpose is to create groups that are similar except for the intervention. If the groups are similar at baseline and treated similarly except for the intervention, differences in outcome are more plausibly caused by the intervention.

RCTs are often called the gold standard for causal inference in medicine.

But even RCTs are not perfect. They can be poorly designed. They can be too small. They can use irrelevant endpoints. They can have poor adherence, missing data, or biased outcome assessment. They can enroll highly selected patients and fail to generalize to routine practice. They can be sponsored or analyzed in ways that create subtle biases.

So the phrase “gold standard” should not mean “automatically trustworthy.” It means that, when well designed and well conducted, randomized trials are our strongest tool for answering causal questions about interventions.

At the top of the evidence hierarchy are systematic reviews and meta-analyses.

A systematic review uses a structured process to identify, evaluate, and synthesize all relevant studies on a question. A meta-analysis statistically combines results from multiple studies.

In principle, this can provide stronger evidence than any single trial, because it pools information across studies. But this only works if the included studies are relevant and high quality. A meta-analysis of biased or heterogeneous studies can produce a precise-looking but misleading answer.

So the hierarchy is useful, but we need nuance.

A well-conducted observational study can be more informative than a poorly conducted randomized trial. A large pragmatic trial may be more useful for real-world decision-making than a small explanatory trial. A systematic review is only as good as the evidence it includes.

The purpose of the evidence hierarchy is not to memorize a pyramid. The purpose is to ask: how likely is this evidence to support a causal conclusion?

Let us now connect this back to randomization.

Why did randomization become such a central idea?

Imagine that a doctor decides who receives a new treatment. The doctor might give it to patients who are more severe because they need it most. Or the doctor might give it to healthier patients because they are more likely to tolerate it. Or the doctor might give it to patients who ask for it, who may be more educated or more engaged. Any of these patterns creates differences between treated and untreated patients.

If treated patients do better, we do not know whether the treatment helped or whether the treated patients were different from the beginning.

Randomization removes the physician’s choice from treatment assignment. It uses chance. This does not guarantee perfect balance, especially in small studies, but it prevents systematic allocation based on prognosis, severity, motivation, physician preference, or other factors.

Randomization balances both known and unknown confounders on average.

This is crucial. Statistical adjustment in observational studies can adjust for measured confounders. But it cannot adjust for confounders that were not measured or not known. Randomization, when successful, can balance even variables we did not think to measure.

That is why randomization is so powerful.

Now, randomization did not emerge because people loved statistics for its own sake. It emerged because medicine needed a way to make fair comparisons.

A fair comparison is the heart of clinical trial design.

When we ask whether a treatment works, we are asking whether outcomes are better than they would have been under a relevant alternative. That alternative could be placebo, no treatment, standard of care, another active treatment, or a different dose.

A trial without a meaningful comparison may produce data, but the data may not answer the question we care about.

Let us now move to the modern clinical trial ecosystem.

Clinical trials today are not just scientific experiments. They are complex systems involving many stakeholders, documents, regulations, technologies, incentives, and responsibilities.

The first key stakeholder is the sponsor.

The sponsor is the organization or person responsible for initiating, financing, and overseeing the trial. In industry, the sponsor is often a pharmaceutical company, biotech company, or medical device company. In academic research, the sponsor may be a university, hospital, investigator, or public funding body.

The sponsor is responsible for the overall trial. That includes developing the protocol, funding the trial, selecting vendors, interacting with regulators, ensuring quality systems, and ultimately owning the data and submission strategy.

It is important to understand that “sponsor” does not necessarily mean “the person paying in a casual sense.” In clinical research, sponsor has a formal meaning. It refers to the party with regulatory responsibility for the study.

The second stakeholder is the investigator.

The investigator is usually a physician or qualified healthcare professional responsible for conducting the trial at a clinical site. The investigator recruits patients, obtains informed consent, ensures that study procedures are followed, oversees patient safety, and ensures that data are collected correctly.

In a multicenter trial, there may be many investigators at many sites. There may also be a principal investigator who has overall scientific leadership, especially in academic studies.

The third stakeholder is the clinical site.

The site is where the trial is actually conducted. This could be a university hospital, academic medical center, private clinic, community hospital, specialist practice, or research unit.

Sites are crucial because they interact with patients directly. A beautifully designed trial can fail if sites cannot recruit patients, follow the protocol, or collect high-quality data.

This is something people outside the industry often underestimate. Clinical trials are not conducted in an abstract scientific space. They are conducted in busy hospitals and clinics, by staff who may also be managing routine patient care, administrative burden, limited resources, and competing studies.

The fourth stakeholder is the CRO, or Contract Research Organization.

A CRO is a company that conducts trial-related activities on behalf of a sponsor.

CROs are central to the clinical trial industry. Many pharmaceutical and biotech companies outsource substantial parts of trial execution to CROs. A sponsor may design the overall development strategy and own the product, while the CRO helps run the operational machinery.

CRO functions can include project management, site selection, site activation, clinical monitoring, data management, biostatistics, medical writing, regulatory affairs, pharmacovigilance, and quality assurance.

In practical terms, if you speak with someone in management at a CRO, they are often concerned with timelines, recruitment, site performance, data quality, regulatory compliance, staffing, budgets, and client relationships. But the underlying scientific purpose remains the same: generate reliable evidence about safety and efficacy.

The fifth stakeholder is the regulator.

Regulators evaluate whether medical products should be approved, whether trials are acceptable, and whether safety is being protected. Examples include the U.S. Food and Drug Administration, the European Medicines Agency, and Swissmedic.

Regulators do not simply ask, “Does the p-value look good?” They evaluate the totality of evidence. They consider trial design, endpoints, patient population, statistical analysis, safety data, manufacturing quality, benefit-risk balance, and the medical context.

A treatment for a mild condition with many existing therapies may need a very favorable safety profile. A treatment for a life-threatening disease with no alternatives may be accepted with greater uncertainty or higher risk. So regulatory judgment is not purely mechanical. It is scientific, clinical, statistical, and ethical.

The sixth stakeholder is the ethics committee or institutional review board.

Ethics committees review clinical research to protect participants. They evaluate whether risks are reasonable, whether informed consent is adequate, whether vulnerable populations are protected, and whether the study is ethically justified.

This is important because clinical trials involve humans. Participants are not just data sources. They are people who may be sick, anxious, hopeful, vulnerable, or desperate for treatment. Clinical research must never treat them merely as instruments for generating evidence.

The seventh stakeholder is the patient.

Historically, patients were often described as subjects. Today, clinical research increasingly emphasizes patient involvement, patient-centered outcomes, quality of life, patient-reported outcomes, and the burden of trial participation.

This shift matters. A trial can measure a biomarker that looks impressive but may not reflect what patients actually care about. Patients may care about pain, fatigue, mobility, independence, survival, cognitive function, ability to work, or avoiding hospital visits. Modern clinical research increasingly asks: are we measuring outcomes that matter to patients?

Now let us return to the big picture.

Why do clinical trials exist?

They exist because medicine is uncertain.

They exist because patients improve and worsen for many reasons.

They exist because physicians can be fooled by experience.

They exist because biological theories can be wrong.

They exist because confounding can make ineffective treatments look effective.

They exist because placebo effects can mimic treatment effects.

They exist because natural disease progression can be mistaken for therapeutic benefit.

They exist because regulators, clinicians, payers, and patients need reliable evidence before adopting interventions widely.

Let us now walk through an integrated example.

Imagine a small biotech company develops a new oral drug for chronic fatigue associated with a particular inflammatory disease. The biological rationale is strong. The drug reduces an inflammatory marker in animal models. Early lab data look promising. A few patients receive the drug through compassionate use, and several report feeling much better.

The company is excited. The physicians are excited. Patients are excited.

But what do we actually know?

We know there is biological plausibility. We know some patients improved after taking the drug. We may know the drug affects a biomarker. But we do not yet know whether the drug improves clinical outcomes compared with what would have happened otherwise.

Chronic fatigue fluctuates. Patients seeking experimental treatment may be highly motivated. Patients may report improvement because they expect improvement. Physicians may interpret changes favorably because they believe in the mechanism. The early patients may not represent the broader population. Maybe the biomarker changes but symptoms do not. Maybe the drug helps only a subgroup. Maybe it has rare safety risks that the early experience is too small to detect.

So the next step is not simply to declare success.

The next step is to design a study that can separate true effect from noise.

That means defining a population, intervention, comparator, outcomes, and time frame. It means deciding whether randomization is needed. It means choosing endpoints that matter. It means controlling bias. It means collecting safety data. It means obtaining ethics approval. It means ensuring informed consent. It means monitoring data quality. It means eventually interpreting results in context.

This is the clinical trial mindset.

Clinical trials are often expensive, slow, and frustrating. But the alternative is worse: adopting treatments based on unreliable evidence and exposing patients to ineffective or harmful interventions.

Now, one subtle point: clinical trials are not only about proving that treatments work. They are also about discovering that they do not work.

A negative trial can be valuable. It can prevent wasted resources, prevent patient harm, redirect research, and correct mistaken assumptions. In industry, a negative trial can be financially painful. But scientifically and ethically, learning that a treatment does not work is still knowledge.

This is important because the public often sees failed trials as pure failure. But from the perspective of evidence generation, a well-designed negative trial has value. It reduces uncertainty.

That is why we can think of clinical research as controlled uncertainty reduction.

At the beginning, uncertainty is high. Does the drug work? Is it safe? Which patients benefit? What dose is appropriate? What outcomes improve? What risks appear over time?

Clinical development is a process of reducing that uncertainty step by step.

Preclinical studies reduce some uncertainty before human testing. Phase I trials reduce uncertainty about safety and dosing. Phase II trials reduce uncertainty about early efficacy and dose selection. Phase III trials reduce uncertainty about benefit and risk in larger populations. Post-marketing studies reduce uncertainty about long-term safety and real-world use.

But the foundation of all of that is today’s central idea: uncontrolled observation is not enough.

Let us spend a little more time on placebo effects, because they are often misunderstood.

When people hear “placebo,” they sometimes think it means the patient imagined the improvement. That is not accurate. Placebo effects can involve real changes in symptoms and experience. Pain, nausea, fatigue, anxiety, sleep, and subjective wellbeing can all be influenced by expectation, context, clinician interaction, and the meaning of treatment.

But placebo effects do not mean every disease can be cured by belief. A placebo may improve subjective pain, but it will not shrink a bacterial infection in the way an antibiotic can. It may improve perceived wellbeing, but it will not reliably reverse a blocked coronary artery. Different outcomes are more or less placebo-sensitive.

This matters for trial design.

If the outcome is subjective, such as pain, fatigue, depression symptoms, or quality of life, blinding and placebo control become especially important.

If the outcome is objective, such as death, stroke, or lab-confirmed infection, placebo effects may be less direct, although bias can still enter through diagnosis, follow-up, adherence, and co-interventions.

This is why endpoint selection matters, which we will discuss more in Lecture 2.

Another important concept is the difference between efficacy and effectiveness.

Efficacy asks: can the treatment work under ideal or controlled conditions?

Effectiveness asks: does the treatment work in real-world practice?

Randomized trials often focus on efficacy, especially earlier in development. They may use strict inclusion criteria, careful monitoring, highly selected patients, and controlled procedures. This helps determine whether the treatment has a true biological or clinical effect.

But after approval, real-world use may be messier. Patients may be older, have multiple diseases, take other medications, miss doses, or use the treatment differently. So a treatment that works in a trial may have a smaller benefit in practice. Or, occasionally, real-world evidence may reveal benefits or harms not captured in trials.

This is part of why clinical research does not end when a product is approved.

Now let us discuss another common misunderstanding.

People sometimes think the purpose of a clinical trial is to produce certainty. But that is not quite right. Clinical trials reduce uncertainty; they do not eliminate it.

Even a large, well-conducted trial gives an estimate. It tells us what happened in a particular population, under particular conditions, with particular endpoints, over a particular time period. There is always uncertainty about generalization, rare harms, long-term effects, subgroups, adherence, and implementation.

This does not make trials weak. It makes them honest.

Good clinical research does not say, “We know everything.” It says, “Given the available evidence, here is what we can reasonably conclude, here is how uncertain we remain, and here is what still needs to be studied.”

That attitude is central to evidence-based medicine.

Evidence-based medicine does not mean blindly following trial results. It means integrating the best available evidence with clinical expertise and patient values.

A trial may show that a drug reduces risk on average. But an individual patient may have different preferences, comorbidities, risk tolerance, or treatment goals. Clinical decision-making still requires judgment. Trials inform decisions; they do not replace them.

Let us now revisit the evidence hierarchy with examples.

Expert opinion: A senior oncologist believes a new treatment approach is promising based on experience and mechanism. This can generate a hypothesis, but it does not establish effectiveness.

Case report: One patient with a rare tumor responds dramatically to an experimental drug. This may be very important, especially if the response is biologically plausible and unusual. It may lead to further research. But it is still one patient.

Case series: Ten patients receive the drug, and six respond. More interesting, but still no control group. Were these patients selected? Was the response assessed consistently? Would some have improved anyway?

Observational cohort: Researchers compare patients who received the drug with similar patients who did not. This is stronger, especially if carefully adjusted, but confounding remains possible.

Randomized trial: Eligible patients are randomly assigned to drug or control. Now the comparison is more credible.

Systematic review and meta-analysis: Multiple randomized trials are combined. This may provide more precise evidence, but only if the trials are comparable and high quality.

The important point is that each level tries to improve the credibility of the comparison.

Medicine is always asking: compared to what?

A treatment that improves symptoms in 60% of patients sounds good. But if 55% improve on placebo, the specific treatment effect may be small. A treatment that reduces mortality from 40% to 30% may sound less dramatic than “60% improved,” but it may be far more clinically meaningful. A drug that improves a biomarker may be promising, but if it does not improve how patients feel, function, or survive, its value may be limited.

This is why clinical trial literacy is so important in industry.

When people in pharma, biotech, CROs, hospitals, and regulators discuss a trial, they are not just discussing whether something “worked.” They ask:

What was the population?

What was the comparator?

Was it randomized?

Was it blinded?

What was the primary endpoint?

Was the endpoint clinically meaningful?

How large was the effect?

How precise was the estimate?

Were there safety concerns?

Was recruitment feasible?

Were there protocol deviations?

Does the result generalize?

Is the evidence sufficient for regulators?

Is the evidence meaningful for clinicians and patients?

These are the questions that structure the entire industry.

Now let us briefly talk about ethics.

Clinical trials create ethical tension because they involve uncertainty. If we already knew a treatment was superior, it might be unethical to randomize some patients away from it. If we already knew it was ineffective or harmful, it would be unethical to test it. Trials are ethically justified when there is genuine uncertainty and when the potential value of the knowledge justifies the risks and burdens to participants.

This is sometimes called clinical equipoise, although the concept can be debated. The basic idea is that the medical community does not yet know which intervention is better.

Informed consent is part of this ethical structure. Participants must understand that research is not the same as ordinary care. In ordinary care, the physician’s sole goal is to treat the individual patient. In research, there is also a goal of generating generalizable knowledge. These goals can align, but they are not identical.

A patient in a trial may receive close monitoring and potential access to new therapies, but they may also receive placebo, experience unknown risks, or undergo extra procedures. Consent must make this clear.

This is especially important in serious diseases, where patients may be desperate for options. Hope is powerful, but hope can also make people vulnerable. Ethical clinical research must protect patients while still allowing progress.

Let us now discuss the language of “subjects” versus “participants.”

Older clinical research language often referred to people in trials as subjects. That term is still used in some regulations and documents. But many people now prefer participants, because it emphasizes that people are actively contributing to research, not merely being studied.

This language shift reflects a broader change toward patient-centered research. Increasingly, patients help shape research priorities, endpoint selection, consent materials, recruitment strategies, and acceptable burden.

For example, a trial might require frequent hospital visits, invasive tests, long questionnaires, or wearable devices. From a sponsor’s perspective, these are data collection tools. From a patient’s perspective, they are burdens. If the burden is too high, recruitment and retention suffer, and the trial may become less representative.

So patient-centeredness is not only ethically good. It can also improve trial quality.

Now, before we conclude, let us synthesize everything through one final scenario.

A physician says:

“I have treated 50 patients with my new method, and 45 improved. This is a 90% success rate.”

What should we ask?

First, what condition were they treating? Some conditions improve naturally. A 90% improvement rate means different things in self-limiting back pain than in metastatic cancer.

Second, how were patients selected? Were these all patients, or only those likely to respond? Were severe cases excluded? Were patients who dropped out counted?

Third, what does “improved” mean? Was it a validated outcome measure, a physician impression, a patient report, a biomarker, or something else?

Fourth, compared to what? What would have happened without treatment? Was there a control group?

Fifth, was the assessment blinded? Did the physician know who received treatment? Did patients know what to expect?

Sixth, how long was follow-up? Did improvement persist or disappear after two weeks?

Seventh, were adverse events measured? A treatment can improve symptoms but cause unacceptable harm.

Eighth, were all results reported? Or only the successful cases?

Ninth, is there biological plausibility? This is not sufficient, but it can matter.

Tenth, has the result been replicated?

This is how someone trained in clinical trials thinks. Not dismissively, but systematically.

The goal is not to reject new treatments. The goal is to avoid fooling ourselves.

So let us end with the central narrative of today’s lecture.

Humans are poor at determining causation through observation alone. We are impressed by improvement after treatment, but improvement can occur for many reasons. Medicine is filled with noise, bias, placebo effects, natural recovery, confounding, and random variation. Clinical trials were developed to manage these problems. Randomized controlled trials are powerful because they create fair comparisons. But RCTs are part of a broader evidence ecosystem that includes case reports, observational studies, systematic reviews, real-world evidence, and post-market surveillance.

Modern clinical research requires many stakeholders: sponsors, investigators, sites, CROs, regulators, ethics committees, and patients. Each plays a different role, but all are part of the same larger mission: generating reliable evidence about whether interventions help or harm people.

The key sentence to remember from today is this:

**Improvement after treatment does not imply improvement because of treatment.**

If you understand that sentence deeply, you understand why clinical trials exist.
