For a **5-hour crash course**, I would optimize for a single outcome:

> Students should understand how a clinical trial moves from an idea to evidence that changes medical practice, and be able to intelligently discuss trials with people working in pharma, biotech, CROs, hospitals, regulators, and health-tech companies.

The biggest mistake would be spending too much time on statistics or disease biology. Most industry conversations revolve around **trial design, endpoints, operations, evidence generation, regulation, and interpretation**.

# Crash Course in Clinical Trials

## 5 Lectures × 60 Minutes

---

# Lecture 1 — Why Clinical Trials Exist

## Theme

**Medicine is difficult because humans are noisy systems.**

---

## Learning Goals

Students should understand:

- why clinical trials are needed
- why intuition often fails in medicine
- what evidence-based medicine means
- how bias enters research

---

## Part 1 — Why Doctors Can Be Wrong

Historical examples:

- bloodletting
- hormone replacement therapy
- anti-arrhythmic drugs
- observational studies gone wrong

Core lesson:

Good intentions ≠ good evidence.

---

## Part 2 — Sources of Error

Introduce:

### Placebo effect

Patients improve because they expect improvement.

### Natural recovery

Many diseases improve without intervention.

### Regression to the mean

Extreme values tend to become less extreme.

### Confounding

Example:

Coffee drinkers appear to have higher mortality because smokers drink more coffee.

### Selection bias

People receiving treatment differ from those who don't.

---

## Part 3 — Evidence Hierarchy

Introduce:

- case reports
- case series
- observational studies
- randomized trials
- systematic reviews
- meta-analysis

Explain strengths and weaknesses.

---

## Part 4 — The Logic of Causal Inference

Core question:

"What would have happened to the same patient if they had not received treatment?"

The fundamental problem:

You never observe both worlds.

Therefore:

- control groups
- randomization

---

## Part 5 — Modern Clinical Research Ecosystem

Who is involved?

### Sponsors

Pharma
Biotech
Medical device companies

### Investigators

Hospitals
Universities

### CROs

Contract Research Organizations

### Regulators

- FDA
- EMA
- Swissmedic

### Patients

The most important stakeholder.

# What Students Should Know After 5 Hours

If the course succeeds, students should comfortably understand and discuss:

- randomized controlled trials
- placebo effects
- confounding and bias
- endpoints and surrogate endpoints
- Phase I–IV trials
- internal vs external validity
- confidence intervals
- hazard ratios and survival analysis
- GCP
- CROs and sponsor responsibilities
- recruitment challenges
- protocol design
- regulatory agencies
- medical devices vs drugs
- real-world evidence
- decentralized trials
- adaptive trials
- AI in clinical research

That knowledge is sufficient to have meaningful conversations with project managers, clinical operations staff, biostatisticians, clinical scientists, regulatory affairs specialists, and mid-level leadership at a CRO or pharma company, while also providing a solid foundation for deeper study later.
