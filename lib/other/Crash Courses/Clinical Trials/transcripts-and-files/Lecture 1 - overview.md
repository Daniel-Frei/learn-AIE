# Lecture 1 — Why Clinical Trials Exist

**Duration:** 60 minutes

**Theme:** _Medicine is difficult because humans are noisy systems._

---

# Learning Objectives

By the end of this lecture, students should be able to:

1. Explain why clinical trials are necessary.
2. Distinguish correlation from causation.
3. Identify major sources of bias in medical research.
4. Understand why randomization emerged historically.
5. Describe the hierarchy of clinical evidence.
6. Explain the roles of sponsors, investigators, CROs, regulators, and patients.
7. Understand the fundamental problem of causal inference that underlies all clinical trials.

# Part 1 — Why Medicine Needs Clinical Trials

## Learning Goal

Students should understand why medicine cannot rely solely on expert opinion, biological plausibility, or clinical experience.

---

## Opening Question

Ask students:

> How do we know whether a treatment actually works?

Most people will answer:

- doctors observe patients
- symptoms improve
- patients report feeling better

This seems reasonable.

The problem is that these observations are often misleading.

---

## Example: The Headache Problem

Suppose:

100 people develop headaches.

A doctor gives everyone a new herbal pill.

After three days:

80 people feel better.

Question:

> Did the pill work?

Not necessarily.

Possible explanations:

- headaches often resolve naturally
- placebo effects
- regression to the mean
- coincidence

The observed improvement does not prove causation.

---

## Core Principle

Introduce:

> Improvement after treatment does not imply improvement because of treatment.

This principle motivates essentially all of clinical trial methodology.

---

## Historical Context

For most of medical history:

Medicine relied on:

- authority
- tradition
- anecdotal experience

Examples:

### Bloodletting

Used for almost 2000 years.

Believed to balance bodily humors.

Often harmed patients.

---

### Early Surgery

Many procedures were adopted before rigorous evaluation.

Some were later shown to be ineffective.

---

### Hormone Replacement Therapy

Observational studies suggested large cardiovascular benefits.

Later randomized trials showed increased risks.

This became one of the most influential examples demonstrating the dangers of relying on observational evidence alone.

---

## Key Takeaway

Medicine requires systematic methods for separating:

- true treatment effects
- random variation
- bias
- natural disease progression

Clinical trials are the primary tool for doing this.

# Part 2 — Why Human Intuition Fails

## Learning Goal

Students should understand why clinicians and researchers are vulnerable to systematic errors in judgment.

---

## Humans Are Pattern-Detection Machines

Evolution favored finding patterns.

Unfortunately:

Humans detect patterns even when none exist.

Medical decision-making is therefore highly susceptible to cognitive biases.

---

## Cognitive Bias Example

Imagine:

A physician gives Drug A to 10 patients.

Eight improve.

The physician concludes:

> Drug A works.

The problem:

The physician never observes:

- what would have happened without treatment
- whether those patients were already recovering

---

## Correlation vs Causation

Introduce:

### Correlation

Two variables move together.

### Causation

One variable directly influences another.

---

### Example

Patients taking a medication have higher mortality.

Possible interpretations:

1. Medication causes death.
2. Sicker patients are more likely to receive medication.

This second explanation is called:

### Confounding

A central concept in clinical research.

---

## Real Example

Observational studies once suggested:

People who took vitamins had lower mortality.

Later evidence suggested:

Vitamin users tended to:

- exercise more
- smoke less
- have higher incomes
- seek healthcare more frequently

The apparent benefit was largely explained by confounding.

---

## The Fundamental Question

Introduce:

> What would have happened to this exact patient if they had not received treatment?

This is impossible to observe.

The same patient cannot simultaneously:

- receive treatment
- not receive treatment

This problem drives the need for control groups and randomization.

# Part 3 — Sources of Bias and Error

## Learning Goal

Students should become familiar with the most common threats to validity.

---

## Placebo Effect

### Definition

Improvement resulting from expectations rather than the treatment itself.

---

### Example

Patient receives:

- sugar pill
- believes it is effective

Symptoms improve.

The improvement may be genuine despite no pharmacological effect.

---

### Why It Matters

If placebo effects exist:

We need comparison groups.

---

## Natural History of Disease

Many conditions improve without intervention.

Examples:

- viral infections
- back pain
- headaches

Without controls:

Natural recovery may be mistaken for treatment efficacy.

---

## Regression to the Mean

One of the most important concepts in clinical research.

---

### Example

Patients enroll when symptoms are unusually severe.

Because symptoms fluctuate naturally:

Many will improve regardless of treatment.

This can create the illusion of efficacy.

---

## Selection Bias

Occurs when groups differ systematically.

Example:

Patients who volunteer for trials may differ from the general population.

They may be:

- healthier
- more motivated
- more health-conscious

---

## Observer Bias

Researchers may unconsciously influence assessments.

Example:

If a physician knows which patient received the experimental drug, they may evaluate outcomes differently.

This motivates blinding.

---

## Publication Bias

Studies with positive results are more likely to be published.

Result:

The scientific literature may overestimate treatment effects.

This becomes important later when discussing meta-analysis.

---

## Discussion Exercise

Ask:

> If a treatment appears successful in 80% of patients, what alternative explanations could exist besides true efficacy?

Students should now be able to identify several possibilities.

# Part 4 — The Evidence Hierarchy

## Learning Goal

Students should understand why some forms of evidence are more reliable than others.

---

## Pyramid of Evidence

Introduce the hierarchy.

Lowest:

### Expert Opinion

Valuable but vulnerable to bias.

---

### Case Reports

Description of one patient.

Useful for:

- rare diseases
- unexpected events

Weak for establishing causation.

---

### Case Series

Multiple patients.

Still lacks controls.

---

### Observational Studies

Examples:

- cohort studies
- case-control studies

Strengths:

- cheaper
- faster
- useful for rare outcomes

Weaknesses:

- confounding

---

### Randomized Controlled Trials (RCTs)

Gold standard for causal inference.

Randomization helps balance:

- known confounders
- unknown confounders

---

### Systematic Reviews and Meta-Analysis

Combine evidence across studies.

Can provide the strongest available evidence.

Provided the underlying studies are high quality.

---

## Important Nuance

The hierarchy is not absolute.

Sometimes:

A well-conducted observational study is more informative than a poor randomized trial.

Students should avoid simplistic thinking.

# Part 5 — The Modern Clinical Trial Ecosystem

## Learning Goal

Students should understand the major stakeholders involved in modern clinical research.

---

## Sponsor

The organization funding and overseeing the trial.

Examples:

- pharmaceutical companies
- biotech startups
- medical device companies
- academic institutions

Responsibilities:

- protocol development
- financing
- regulatory interactions

---

## Investigators

Physicians responsible for conducting the study at sites.

Responsibilities:

- recruiting patients
- obtaining consent
- collecting data

---

## Clinical Sites

Hospitals

Academic medical centers

Private clinics

Locations where patients participate.

---

## CROs (Contract Research Organizations)

A central industry concept.

Definition:

Organizations that conduct trial activities on behalf of sponsors.

Common functions:

- project management
- monitoring
- data management
- statistics
- regulatory affairs

Many pharmaceutical companies outsource substantial portions of trial operations to CROs.

---

## Regulators

Examples:

- U.S. Food and Drug Administration
- European Medicines Agency
- Swissmedic

Responsibilities:

- evaluating evidence
- approving therapies
- ensuring patient safety

---

## Ethics Committees / Institutional Review Boards

Independent oversight bodies.

Responsibilities:

- patient protection
- ethical review
- informed consent review

---

## Patients

Patients are not merely study subjects.

Modern clinical research increasingly emphasizes:

- patient-centered outcomes
- quality of life
- patient-reported outcomes

---

# End-of-Lecture Synthesis

Conclude with the central narrative:

1. Humans are poor at determining causation through observation alone.
2. Medicine is filled with bias, noise, and uncertainty.
3. Clinical trials were developed to overcome these limitations.
4. Randomized controlled trials are our best tool for causal inference.
5. Modern clinical research requires coordination among sponsors, investigators, CROs, regulators, ethics committees, and patients.

---

# Recommended Final Discussion Question

> A physician develops a new treatment and reports that 90% of their patients improved after receiving it. What additional information would you need before concluding that the treatment works?

If students can answer using concepts such as:

- control groups
- placebo effects
- confounding
- randomization
- natural recovery
- selection bias

then they have achieved the primary learning objectives of Lecture 1.
