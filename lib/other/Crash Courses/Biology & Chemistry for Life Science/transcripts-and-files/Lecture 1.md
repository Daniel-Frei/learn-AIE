# Lecture 1 - Chemistry of Life

## Theme

**Living systems are constrained chemistry.**

## Meta-Goal

Students should leave with a mechanistic picture of life:

> Cells, proteins, membranes, genes, metabolism, disease, drugs, and evolution all depend on molecules interacting under chemical and physical constraints.

This lecture builds the chemical intuition needed for every later lecture. It should not become a general chemistry course. Teach the chemistry that explains living systems.

---

## Learning Objectives

By the end, students should understand:

- why carbon, hydrogen, nitrogen, oxygen, phosphorus, and sulfur are central to life
- why carbon's four covalent bonds support complex organic molecules
- how shape, charge, polarity, and weak interactions determine molecular behavior
- why water is not passive background but the medium that shapes life
- why hydrophobic effects drive membranes and protein folding
- how monomers, polymers, hydrolysis, and dehydration reactions connect food, cells, and macromolecules
- what carbohydrates, lipids, proteins, and nucleic acids do
- why protein sequence, folding, dynamics, active sites, and specificity matter
- how ATP, enzymes, gradients, and metabolism let cells maintain order

## Section 1 - Why Chemistry Matters

Core message:

> Biology is chemistry organized by evolution.

Examples:

- drugs bind proteins
- viruses bind host-cell receptors
- antibodies recognize molecular surfaces
- DNA stores information in chemical sequence
- proteins fold because of chemical interactions
- cells communicate using signaling molecules

Biology feels complex because living systems are:

- noisy
- adaptive
- redundant
- decentralized
- regulated by feedback
- evolved rather than cleanly engineered

Avoid the false dichotomy that biology is either "just chemistry" or "too messy for mechanism." The useful stance is:

> Biology has mechanisms, but those mechanisms operate in probabilistic, evolved, context-dependent systems.

## Section 2 - Atoms, CHNOPS, Carbon, and Bonds

### Atoms and Elements

An atom contains protons, neutrons, and electrons.

- protons help define the element
- neutrons affect isotopes and mass
- electrons determine bonding, charge, and reactivity

The main biological elements are often summarized as **CHNOPS**:

| Element    | Why it matters                                                 |
| ---------- | -------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Carbon     | organic backbones, chains, rings, molecular diversity          |
| Hydrogen   | water, organic molecules, acid-base chemistry, energy transfer |
| Nitrogen   | amino acids, proteins, nucleic acids                           |
| Oxygen     | water, metabolism, many polar groups                           |
| Phosphorus | DNA/RNA backbones, ATP, phospholipids, phosphorylation         |
| Sulfur     | amino acids, protein folding, redox chemistry                  |

### Why Carbon Matters

Carbon can form four covalent bonds. This supports:

- chains
- branches
- rings
- isomers
- stable but flexible organic molecules

Organic molecules contain carbon. In biology, carbohydrates, lipids, proteins, and nucleic acids all rely on carbon-based structures.

### Chemical Bonds and Interactions

Teach four interaction types and what each explains:

| Interaction        | Basic idea                  | Biological role                                          |
| ------------------ | --------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------- |
| Covalent bond      | atoms share electrons       | stable backbones of DNA, proteins, carbohydrates, lipids |
| Ionic interaction  | opposite charges attract    | salts, ion gradients, charged protein regions            |
| Hydrogen bond      | partial charges attract     | water properties, DNA base pairing, protein structure    |
| Hydrophobic effect | nonpolar groups avoid water | membranes, protein cores, molecular organization         |

Important distinction:

- covalent bonds usually form stable molecular skeletons
- weak interactions often determine shape, binding, folding, and regulation

Many weak interactions together can create strong biological specificity.

## Section 3 - Water and the Environment of Life

Water is polar because oxygen pulls shared electrons more strongly than hydrogen. This creates partial charges and enables hydrogen bonding.

Consequences:

### Solubility

Polar and charged substances often dissolve well in water because water can surround and stabilize them. Nonpolar substances dissolve poorly because they cannot form favorable interactions with water.

This explains:

- why salts can dissolve into ions
- why many metabolites and proteins interact with water
- why lipids behave differently from sugars or salts

### Hydrophobic Effect

Nonpolar molecules tend to cluster away from water. This is not because they actively hate water; it is because water-water interactions are more favorable than water-nonpolar interactions.

The hydrophobic effect drives:

- lipid bilayer formation
- membrane compartmentalization
- protein folding, with hydrophobic residues often buried inside

### Water's Physical Properties

Water's hydrogen bonding explains:

- cohesion
- adhesion
- surface tension
- relatively high heat capacity
- ice floating because solid water is less dense than liquid water

For this course, the highest-yield point is that water's polarity and hydrogen bonding shape cellular organization.

### pH

pH reflects hydrogen ion concentration. Biological molecules have groups that can gain or lose protons, changing charge, shape, and binding behavior.

This matters for:

- enzyme function
- protein folding
- blood chemistry
- cellular compartments such as lysosomes

## Section 4 - Biological Macromolecules and Polymer Chemistry

Biology repeatedly builds large molecules from smaller building blocks.

Key terms:

- monomer: small building block
- polymer: chain of repeated building blocks
- macromolecule: large biological molecule
- hydrolysis: uses water to break bonds
- dehydration reaction: removes water while joining building blocks

Hydrolysis helps digestion break food polymers into usable pieces. Dehydration reactions help cells build larger molecules from smaller ones.

### Four Major Classes

| Class         | Building blocks                           | Main roles                                                     |
| ------------- | ----------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Carbohydrates | monosaccharides                           | energy, short-term storage, structure, cell recognition        |
| Lipids        | fatty acids and related hydrophobic units | membranes, long-term energy storage, hormones, signaling       |
| Proteins      | amino acids                               | catalysis, transport, signaling, movement, structure, immunity |
| Nucleic acids | nucleotides                               | genetic information storage and transfer                       |

### Carbohydrates

Glucose is a central fuel. Starch and glycogen store glucose units. Some carbohydrates also provide structure and help cells recognize each other.

### Lipids

Lipids are mostly hydrophobic. Phospholipids are amphipathic: they have hydrophilic heads and hydrophobic tails. This lets them self-assemble into bilayers.

Lipids also include steroid hormones and long-term energy stores.

### Proteins

Proteins are amino acid polymers. Their side chains differ in charge, polarity, hydrophobicity, size, and chemical reactivity. These properties drive folding and interactions.

Protein examples:

- hemoglobin transports oxygen
- insulin signals glucose regulation
- antibodies recognize targets
- enzymes catalyze reactions
- collagen and keratin provide structure

### Nucleic Acids

DNA and RNA are nucleotide polymers. DNA stores relatively stable inherited information. RNA acts as a working copy, messenger, regulator, or tool depending on context.

## Section 5 - Proteins and Enzymes

Core idea:

> Sequence -> structure -> dynamics -> function.

Protein function depends on:

- amino acid sequence
- folding into three-dimensional shape
- chemical properties of side chains
- conformational changes
- binding surfaces and active sites
- cellular context

Avoid overusing a rigid lock-and-key model. Many proteins are dynamic and change shape when binding partners, substrates, or regulators interact with them.

### Enzymes

Enzymes are biological catalysts. They speed reactions by lowering activation energy, but they do not change the overall energy balance of the reaction.

Enzyme specificity comes from:

- shape complementarity
- charge and polarity
- local chemical environment
- induced fit and dynamics

Enzymes make life possible because uncatalyzed reactions would often be too slow for cells.

### Regulation

Proteins can be regulated by:

- ligand binding
- phosphorylation
- pH
- temperature
- concentration of substrates or products
- cellular localization

This prepares students for signaling, pharmacology, and gene regulation.

## Section 6 - ATP, Metabolism, and Non-Equilibrium Life

Cells require constant energy flow. They build, repair, transport, signal, move, and replicate.

ATP is a cellular energy currency. It couples energy-releasing processes to energy-requiring work.

Metabolism includes:

- catabolism: breaking molecules down and releasing usable energy
- anabolism: building molecules and consuming energy

Life is not at equilibrium. Cells maintain organization by using energy and exporting entropy to the environment.

Gradients are stored energy. Later lectures will use gradients to explain membranes, neurons, muscles, mitochondria, pharmacology, and physiology.

---

## Final Synthesis

The big ideas:

1. Biology emerges from molecular interactions.
2. Water shapes biological organization.
3. Weak interactions can create strong specificity when many act together.
4. Macromolecules are built from reusable chemical modules.
5. Proteins connect sequence, structure, dynamics, and function.
6. Life requires continuous energy flow.
