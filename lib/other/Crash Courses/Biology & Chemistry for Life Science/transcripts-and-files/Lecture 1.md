# Lecture 1 — Chemistry of Life

## Theme:

**Living systems are constrained chemistry.**

---

# Meta-Goal of the Lecture

Students should leave with the following mental model:

> Biology is not “magic.”
>
> Living systems are collections of molecules interacting under the laws of chemistry and physics.
>
> Cells, proteins, signaling, drugs, disease, and even evolution all emerge from molecular interactions.

This lecture is meant to create the chemical intuition needed for the rest of the course.

---

# Learning Objectives

By the end of the lecture, students should understand:

- why chemistry is foundational to biology
- how atoms form molecules
- why water dominates biology
- what biological macromolecules are
- why proteins are central to life
- how structure determines function
- how energy flows through biological systems
- why thermodynamics matters in biology

---

# SECTION 1 — Why Chemistry Matters for Biology

## Time: ~5 min

---

# Core Message

> Everything in biology ultimately happens through molecular interactions.

Examples:

- Drugs bind receptors
- Viruses bind cells
- Antibodies bind pathogens
- DNA stores information chemically
- Proteins fold because of chemistry
- Cells communicate using molecules

---

# Framing for Technical Students

Biology can initially seem:

- messy
- overly descriptive
- exception-filled

But underneath that:

> Biology has deep mechanistic structure.

Living systems are:

- dynamic
- stochastic
- self-organizing
- evolved rather than engineered

This creates complexity.

---

# Key Analogy

| Computer Science    | Biology         |
| ------------------- | --------------- |
| Hardware            | Chemistry       |
| Programs            | Gene regulation |
| Networking          | Cell signaling  |
| Distributed systems | Tissues/organs  |
| Optimization        | Evolution       |

---

# Important Clarification

Biology is NOT:

- purely deterministic
- cleanly modular
- centrally controlled

Instead:

- noisy
- probabilistic
- adaptive
- redundant
- decentralized

This becomes important later in:

- medicine
- drug discovery
- clinical trials
- AI for biology

# SECTION 2 — Atoms, Bonds, and Molecules

## Time: ~15 min

---

# Part A — Atoms

## (~4 min)

Very lightweight overview.

Students do NOT need:

- orbital theory
- advanced quantum chemistry

Focus on intuition.

---

# Core Concepts

An atom consists of:

- protons
- neutrons
- electrons

Electrons matter most biologically because they determine:

- bonding
- reactivity
- charge

---

# Important Elements in Biology

Teach the “CHNOPS” framework:

| Element    | Role                          |
| ---------- | ----------------------------- |
| Carbon     | backbone of organic molecules |
| Hydrogen   | water, energy, structure      |
| Nitrogen   | proteins, DNA                 |
| Oxygen     | water, metabolism             |
| Phosphorus | DNA, ATP, membranes           |
| Sulfur     | protein structure             |

---

# Why Carbon Matters

Carbon is foundational because it can form:

- 4 covalent bonds
- stable yet flexible structures
- chains
- rings
- complex architectures

This enables:

- proteins
- DNA
- lipids
- carbohydrates

Key insight:

> Carbon chemistry enables biological complexity.

---

# Part B — Chemical Bonds

## (~6 min)

---

# Covalent Bonds

Shared electrons.

Strong bonds.

Used for:

- DNA backbone
- proteins
- stable molecular structure

---

# Ionic Interactions

Opposite charges attract.

Example:

- Na⁺ and Cl⁻

Biological importance:

- ion gradients
- nerve signaling
- membrane potentials

---

# Hydrogen Bonds

Weak individually, powerful collectively.

Crucial for:

- water properties
- DNA double helix
- protein structure

This is one of the most important concepts in biology.

---

# Hydrophobic Interactions

Extremely important.

Nonpolar molecules avoid water.

This drives:

- membrane formation
- protein folding
- molecular organization

Key insight:

> Much of biology emerges because oil and water separate.

---

# Part C — Molecules and Shape

## (~5 min)

---

# Structure Determines Function

Critical concept.

Examples:

| Molecule   | Function            |
| ---------- | ------------------- |
| Hemoglobin | oxygen transport    |
| Antibody   | target recognition  |
| DNA        | information storage |
| Insulin    | signaling           |

---

# Biological Specificity

Molecules interact because of:

- shape
- charge
- chemistry
- dynamics

Not magic.

---

# Drug Example

A drug works because:

- it fits/binds a target
- alters function

This introduces:

- receptors
- affinity
- specificity

Important later for pharmacology.

# SECTION 3 — Water and the Environment of Life

## Time: ~10 min

---

# Why Water Matters

Students often underestimate this.

But water is arguably:

> the central medium of biology.

---

# Water Is Polar

Oxygen pulls electrons more strongly than hydrogen.

Result:

- partial charges
- hydrogen bonding

---

# Consequences of Water

---

# 1. Solubility

Polar molecules dissolve well.

Nonpolar molecules do not.

This creates biological organization.

---

# 2. Membranes Form Spontaneously

Lipids self-assemble because:

- hydrophobic tails avoid water
- hydrophilic heads interact with water

This creates:

- compartments
- cells
- boundaries

Key insight:

> Cells emerge naturally from chemistry.

---

# 3. Protein Folding

Proteins fold partly because:

- hydrophobic amino acids hide inside
- hydrophilic amino acids remain outside

This creates stable 3D structure.

---

# Entropy Intuition

Do NOT overcomplicate.

Explain intuitively:

Systems tend toward:

- disorder
- energy spreading

Life maintains local order by:

- consuming energy
- exporting entropy

---

# Important Biological Consequence

Biology is:

- dynamic
- energy consuming
- never truly at equilibrium

---

# Overview

| Class         | Main Function                   |

## (~3 min)

- glucose
- starch
- glycogen

Functions:

- energy
- short-term storage
- structural support

- cells run heavily on glucose
- metabolism converts chemical energy

- diabetes
- blood glucose

---

# Part B — Lipids

## (~4 min)

Examples:

- fats
- phospholipids
- cholesterol

Functions:

- membranes
- energy storage
- hormones/signaling

Explain:

- membranes are lipid bilayers
- membranes are dynamic, not rigid

Mention:

- steroid hormones
- cholesterol importance

# Part C — Proteins

## (~5 min)

Most important macromolecule for the course.

Explain:

Proteins are:

- chains of amino acids
- folded into 3D structures
- molecular machines

Functions:

- catalysis
- transport
- structure
- signaling
- movement
- immunity

Examples:

- enzymes
- antibodies
- insulin
- hemoglobin

Key insight:

> Proteins perform most active work inside cells.

# Part D — Nucleic Acids

## (~3 min)

DNA and RNA.

Functions:

- information storage
- information transfer

# SECTION 5 — Proteins and Enzymes

## Time: ~10 min

---

# Why Proteins Matter So Much

Modern biology is heavily protein-centric.

Most drugs target proteins.

Most diseases involve proteins.

Most cellular behavior depends on proteins.

---

# Amino Acids

Very lightweight introduction.

20 standard amino acids.

Different chemical properties:

- hydrophobic
- charged
- polar

These properties determine folding.

---

# Protein Folding

## Critical topic

A protein sequence folds into a 3D structure.

This structure determines:

- binding
- catalysis
- interactions
- function

Key idea:

> Sequence → Structure → Function

This is foundational modern biology.

---

# Enzymes

Enzymes accelerate reactions.

Without enzymes:

- biology would be far too slow

Explain:

- activation energy
- catalysts

---

# Specificity

Enzymes are selective.

This enables:

- regulation
- complex pathways
- metabolic control

---

# Modern AI Connection

Mention briefly:

- AlphaFold
- protein design
- AI drug discovery

Students should see:

> modern AI and biology increasingly converge at the level of molecular structure and function.

---

# SECTION 6 — Energy, ATP, and Metabolism

## Time: ~5 min

---

# Core Idea

Living systems require constant energy flow.

Cells are not static.

They:

- build
- repair
- transport
- communicate
- replicate

All requires energy.

---

# ATP

## The cellular energy currency

Explain simply:

ATP stores usable chemical energy.

Cells use ATP to:

- drive reactions
- transport molecules
- power movement

---

# Metabolism

Very high-level overview.

Two broad categories:

| Type       | Purpose              |
| ---------- | -------------------- |
| Catabolism | break down molecules |
| Anabolism  | build molecules      |

---

# Important Insight

Biology depends heavily on:

- energy gradients
- non-equilibrium dynamics
- continuous chemical flux
