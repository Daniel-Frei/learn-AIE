# Crash Course: Biology & Chemistry for Life Science and Medicine

## Goal of the Course

This course is not meant to turn technically literate students into full biologists, chemists, physicians, or clinical researchers.

The goal is to give students with a math, statistics, computer science, AI, or engineering background a usable mental model of living systems so they can:

- read biomedical papers with less confusion
- reason about diseases and treatments mechanistically
- understand why biological systems are probabilistic and context-dependent
- follow conversations with scientists, clinicians, and biotech teams
- understand where AI can help biology and medicine, and where measurement and evidence are still bottlenecks

The course is organized around one central idea:

> Biology is an information-processing system implemented in chemistry.

Clinical trials and biomedical research methods matter, but in this course they are context: they explain how biomedical claims are tested. They are not the core subject. The core subject is the biology and chemistry needed to understand life science and medicine.

## Curriculum Philosophy

The course prioritizes:

- conceptual compression
- mechanistic reasoning
- systems thinking across molecular, cellular, tissue, and organism scales
- transfer to biomedical papers and practical discussions
- high-quality practice questions that are treated as part of the course material

The course de-emphasizes:

- taxonomy-heavy biology
- advanced organic chemistry mechanisms
- memorizing anatomy lists
- clinical-trial operations as a standalone subject
- trivia that does not help students reason from mechanism to medicine

Students should leave knowing both what they understand and what they still need to look up.

---

## Lecture 0 - Preparation Practice

Lecture 0 is not a lecture. It is a preparation question set.

Its purpose is to give students enough prerequisite vocabulary and simple reasoning practice to attempt Lectures 1-5 without being blocked by basic terms.

Lecture 0 should cover:

- atoms, elements, molecules, ions, bonds, polarity, pH, and water
- organic molecules, monomers, polymers, hydrolysis, and dehydration reactions
- carbohydrates, lipids, proteins, nucleic acids, amino acids, ATP, and enzymes
- cells, membranes, organelles, gradients, receptors, ligands, feedback, and immunity
- DNA, RNA, genes, chromosomes, codons, proteins, mutation, evolution, and gene regulation
- homeostasis, disease categories, drugs, dose response, pharmacokinetics, pharmacodynamics, biomarkers, and basic evidence reasoning

The knowledge difficulty should stay below the lecture question sets. The answer options should still be rigorous: wrong options should be plausible same-topic confusions, not easy-to-eliminate jokes or unrelated categories.

## Lecture 1 - Chemistry of Life

## Theme

**Living systems are constrained chemistry.**

## Learning Goals

Students should understand:

- why chemistry is foundational to biology
- how CHNOPS elements support biological molecules
- why carbon can build complex organic molecules
- how covalent bonds, ionic interactions, hydrogen bonds, polarity, and hydrophobic effects shape biology
- why water is the medium of life
- how monomers, polymers, hydrolysis, and dehydration reactions connect food, macromolecules, and cells
- what carbohydrates, lipids, proteins, and nucleic acids do
- why protein sequence, folding, shape, and dynamics determine function
- why enzymes and ATP make life possible

## Sections

| Section | Topic                                                | Time   |
| ------- | ---------------------------------------------------- | ------ |
| 1       | Why chemistry matters for life                       | 5 min  |
| 2       | Atoms, CHNOPS, carbon, and chemical bonds            | 12 min |
| 3       | Water, polarity, solubility, and hydrophobic effects | 12 min |
| 4       | Biological macromolecules and polymer chemistry      | 16 min |
| 5       | Proteins, enzymes, and structure-function reasoning  | 10 min |
| 6       | ATP, metabolism, and non-equilibrium life            | 5 min  |

## Core Ideas

- Most biology emerges from many weak interactions acting together in water.
- Carbon's bonding flexibility enables organic molecular diversity.
- Water's polarity explains solubility, membranes, protein folding, cohesion, and many biological reactions.
- The four major classes of biological macromolecules are not vocabulary trivia; they are the materials and information systems from which cells are built.
- Proteins are sequence-to-structure-to-function systems.
- Life requires continuous energy flow and chemical work.

## Lecture 2 - Cells as Information-Processing Systems

## Theme

**Cells are organized, self-regulating chemical systems.**

## Learning Goals

Students should understand:

- why cells need boundaries and compartments
- how prokaryotic and eukaryotic cells differ at a useful high level
- how organelles divide labor without acting as isolated modules
- how lipid bilayers, selective permeability, diffusion, osmosis, channels, pumps, and gradients support life
- how receptors, ligands, signaling cascades, amplification, and feedback let cells process information
- how cell division and programmed cell death preserve tissues
- why cancer can be understood as dysregulated cellular evolution
- how innate and adaptive immunity coordinate defense

## Sections

| Section | Topic                                                  | Time   |
| ------- | ------------------------------------------------------ | ------ |
| 1       | What a cell solves: boundaries, information, resources | 6 min  |
| 2       | Cell architecture and organelles                       | 14 min |
| 3       | Membranes, transport, osmosis, and gradients           | 12 min |
| 4       | Cell signaling, amplification, and feedback            | 14 min |
| 5       | Division, apoptosis, stem cells, and cancer intuition  | 8 min  |
| 6       | Innate and adaptive immunity overview                  | 6 min  |

## Core Ideas

- A cell is not a bag of molecules; it is organized chemistry.
- Membranes create compartments and gradients, and gradients store usable energy.
- Cells sense and respond through receptor-mediated signaling.
- Feedback loops stabilize or amplify biological responses.
- Tissue health depends on balancing growth, repair, specialization, and cell death.
- Immune defense is distributed recognition and response, not a single organ.

## Lecture 3 - Genetics, Proteins, and Biological Regulation

## Theme

**Biology is programmable, but probabilistic and regulated.**

## Learning Goals

Students should understand:

- what DNA, genes, chromosomes, and genomes are
- why genes are not deterministic blueprints
- how complementary base pairing enables copying and repair
- how the central dogma connects DNA, RNA, and proteins
- how codons and ribosomes translate nucleotide sequences into amino acid chains
- why gene expression and regulation matter more than gene count
- how transcription factors, chromatin, and epigenetic marks influence access to information
- how mutation, selection, drift, and evolution affect medicine
- why sequencing, plasmids, recombinant DNA, CRISPR, mRNA technology, and gene therapy are possible

## Sections

| Section | Topic                                                           | Time   |
| ------- | --------------------------------------------------------------- | ------ |
| 1       | DNA, genes, chromosomes, and genomes                            | 12 min |
| 2       | Replication, transcription, translation, and codons             | 16 min |
| 3       | Gene expression, regulatory networks, and cell identity         | 14 min |
| 4       | Mutation, evolution, drift, selection, and medical relevance    | 10 min |
| 5       | Biotechnology: sequencing, plasmids, CRISPR, mRNA, gene therapy | 8 min  |

## Core Ideas

- DNA stores inheritable sequence information, but cellular behavior depends on how that information is used.
- RNA is a working copy, messenger, regulator, or tool depending on context.
- Proteins execute much of biology because they bind, catalyze, signal, transport, and build structures.
- Cell types differ mostly because they express different gene programs.
- Evolution is active in infections, cancer, antibiotic resistance, and viral immune escape.
- Modern biotechnology works because biology has readable, writable, and editable information layers.

## Lecture 4 - Physiology, Disease, and Pharmacology

## Theme

**Disease is disrupted regulation across scales.**

## Learning Goals

Students should understand:

- how cells, tissues, organs, and organ systems coordinate
- how homeostasis uses sensing, comparison, and response
- why physiology is distributed regulation rather than central command
- how major disease categories represent different failures of regulation
- why symptoms can arise from host responses as well as direct damage
- how drugs interact with targets, receptors, enzymes, transporters, and pathways
- how agonists, antagonists, dose response, therapeutic window, pharmacokinetics, and pharmacodynamics fit together
- why side effects and patient variation are expected in complex systems
- how biomarkers and precision medicine connect measurement to treatment choices

## Sections

| Section | Topic                                                 | Time   |
| ------- | ----------------------------------------------------- | ------ |
| 1       | Physiology as multi-scale coordination                | 12 min |
| 2       | Homeostasis and feedback control                      | 10 min |
| 3       | Disease categories as regulatory failures             | 14 min |
| 4       | Pharmacology fundamentals                             | 16 min |
| 5       | Patient variation, biomarkers, and precision medicine | 8 min  |

## Core Ideas

- Health is active regulation, not passive normality.
- Diseases often arise from interacting networks, not one isolated broken part.
- Drugs perturb systems and often affect more than the intended endpoint.
- PK asks what the body does to the drug; PD asks what the drug does to the body.
- Biomarkers are measurements that require validation before they can guide decisions.

## Lecture 5 - Biomedical Systems, Biotechnology, and Evidence

## Theme

**Modern biomedicine connects mechanism, measurement, engineering, and evidence.**

## Why This Replaces the Old Lecture 5

Clinical trials are important, but a one-hour crash course in biology and chemistry should not spend its final lecture mainly on trial design. A separate Clinical Trials Crash Course exists for deeper coverage.

This revised Lecture 5 uses clinical trials and research evidence as context while keeping the core on biological and biomedical understanding:

- pathogens and host defense
- vaccines, antivirals, antibiotics, and resistance
- diagnostics and biomarkers
- biotechnology and drug discovery
- model systems and translational limits
- AI in biology and medicine
- evidence as the final check on biomedical claims

## Learning Goals

Students should understand:

- how infectious disease, immunity, vaccines, antimicrobials, and resistance connect the previous lectures
- why biotechnology often works by reusing cellular machinery
- how diagnostics and biomarkers translate biological states into measurements
- why preclinical systems such as cells, organoids, and animals are useful but limited
- how target discovery, therapeutic modality, safety, efficacy, and patient selection fit together
- why clinical trials are needed to test claims, without treating trial operations as the main course topic
- where AI is useful in biology and medicine, and why biology, measurement, workflow, and evidence still matter

## Sections

| Section | Topic                                                            | Time   |
| ------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------- | ------ |
| 1       | Infectious disease as a systems case study                       | 12 min |
| 2       | Vaccines, antimicrobials, resistance, and immune memory          | 10 min |
| 3       | Biotechnology and therapeutic modalities                         | 12 min |
| 4       | Diagnostics, biomarkers, model systems, and translational limits | 10 min |
| 5       | Clinical evidence as context: controls, randomization, endpoints | 8 min  |
| 6       | AI in modern biomedicine                                         | 8 min  |

## Core Ideas

- Infection is a molecular interaction between pathogen and host, followed by cellular and organism-level responses.
- Vaccines train immune recognition; antibiotics, antivirals, antibodies, small molecules, proteins, mRNA, and gene therapies intervene through different biological mechanisms.
- Resistance evolves because populations vary and selection changes which variants survive.
- Biotechnology is powerful because cells can read, copy, express, and sometimes edit biological information.
- Measurements are not automatically meaningful; biomarkers and diagnostics must connect to mechanism and outcomes.
- Clinical trials reduce uncertainty about safety, efficacy, and benefit, but they are a validation layer over the biological course content.
- AI can accelerate discovery and interpretation, but it does not remove the need for reliable biological understanding and evidence.

---

## What Students Should Know Afterwards

After the course, students should be able to:

- explain life as chemistry, organization, information, regulation, and evolution
- reason from atoms and molecules to cells, tissues, organisms, diseases, and treatments
- distinguish mechanism, measurement, association, and evidence
- understand why medicine is probabilistic
- interpret common biomedical terms without treating them as isolated vocabulary
- identify where clinical trials matter without mistaking clinical-trial design for the core of biology and chemistry
- see how AI and biotech build on biological mechanisms rather than replacing them
