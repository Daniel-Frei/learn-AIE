# Crash Course: Biology & Chemistry for Life Science, Medicine, and Clinical Research

## Goal of the Course

This course is not meant to make students “biologists” or “chemists.”

The goal is to give technically literate students (math/stats/CS/AI background) a **mental model of living systems**, so they can:

- understand biomedical papers
- reason about diseases and treatments
- follow clinical trial discussions
- work with medical researchers
- understand modern biotech / AI-for-healthcare systems
- avoid common conceptual mistakes

The course is designed around a key idea:

> Biology is an information-processing system implemented in chemistry.

This framing works especially well for students coming from mathematics, AI, engineering, or systems thinking.

---

# High-Level Structure

| Lecture | Theme                                             | Core Question                                                    |
| ------- | ------------------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------- |
| 1       | Chemistry of Life                                 | What is biological matter made of and why does chemistry matter? |
| 2       | Cells as Information Systems                      | How do cells work as dynamic computational systems?              |
| 3       | Genetics, Proteins & Regulation                   | How does biological information flow and change?                 |
| 4       | Physiology, Disease & Pharmacology                | How do organisms fail and how do drugs intervene?                |
| 5       | Clinical Trials, Measurement & Modern Biomedicine | How do we generate trustworthy medical knowledge?                |

---

# Philosophy of the Curriculum

The course intentionally prioritizes:

- conceptual compression
- systems thinking
- transfer learning
- reading scientific literature
- mechanistic understanding

over:

- memorization
- taxonomy-heavy biology
- detailed organic chemistry
- excessive anatomy

Students should leave with a framework that allows them to rapidly learn more later.

# Lecture 1 — Chemistry of Life

## Theme:

**Living systems are constrained chemistry.**

---

## Learning Goals

Students should understand:

- why chemistry matters in biology
- how molecules behave in water
- why structure determines function
- how energy flows through biological systems
- the key molecular building blocks of life

---

# Part 1 — Atoms, Bonds, and Molecules (15 min)

Teach only what matters biologically.

## Core concepts

- Atoms
- Electrons
- Chemical bonds
- Molecules
- Charge & polarity

Focus on:

- covalent bonds
- ionic interactions
- hydrogen bonds
- hydrophobic interactions

Emphasize:

> Most biology emerges from weak interactions in water.

---

# Part 2 — Water as the Medium of Life (10 min)

This is foundational and often underestimated.

Explain:

- polarity
- hydrogen bonding
- why water dissolves some molecules but not others
- entropy intuition
- hydrophobic effect

Key insight:

> Cell membranes, protein folding, and much of biology emerge because oil and water do not mix.

---

# Part 3 — The Four Biological Macromolecules (20 min)

## Carbohydrates

Energy + structure

## Lipids

Membranes + signaling + energy storage

## Proteins

The “machines” of biology

## Nucleic acids

Information storage

Do not overdo memorization.

Students should understand:

- structure
- purpose
- why evolution selected these molecules

---

# Part 4 — Proteins and Structure → Function (10 min)

Critical concept.

Explain:

- amino acids
- folding
- active sites
- specificity
- enzymes

Introduce:

- lock-and-key is oversimplified
- proteins are dynamic systems

Connection to AI:

- proteins are sequence → structure → function mappings

Mention:

- AlphaFold
- protein engineering
- modern computational biology

---

# Part 5 — Energy and Metabolism (5 min)

Introduce only the big picture:

- ATP
- energy transfer
- metabolism
- gradients

Key idea:

> Life survives by maintaining low entropy locally using external energy.

---

# Lecture 2 — Cells as Information-Processing Systems

## Theme:

**Cells are dynamic self-regulating chemical computers.**

---

## Learning Goals

Students should understand:

- what cells are
- how cellular organization works
- how signaling and regulation work
- why cells are fundamentally information systems

---

# Part 1 — Cell Architecture (15 min)

Explain:

- membrane
- nucleus
- mitochondria
- ribosomes
- ER/Golgi
- cytoskeleton

Do NOT force memorization.

Instead emphasize:

| Organelle    | Analogy                |
| ------------ | ---------------------- |
| Nucleus      | Information storage    |
| Ribosome     | Compiler/factory       |
| Membrane     | Boundary + routing     |
| Mitochondria | Energy plant           |
| Cytoskeleton | Dynamic infrastructure |

---

# Part 2 — Cell Membranes & Transport (10 min)

Teach:

- lipid bilayer
- diffusion
- osmosis
- ion gradients
- channels/pumps

Key insight:

> Much of physiology is controlled electrical and chemical gradients.

This later helps with:

- neurons
- pharmacology
- disease
- imaging
- signaling

---

# Part 3 — Cell Signaling (15 min)

Critical topic.

Explain:

- receptors
- ligands
- signaling cascades
- amplification
- feedback loops

Show how cells “sense” environments.

Introduce:

- hormones
- neurotransmitters
- cytokines

Connection to systems engineering:

- feedback control
- noisy signaling
- distributed coordination

---

# Part 4 — Cell Division & Death (10 min)

Explain:

- mitosis
- apoptosis
- stem cells
- cancer intuition

Key idea:

> Cancer is fundamentally dysregulated evolution inside tissues.

---

# Part 5 — Immune System Overview (10 min)

Very high leverage topic.

Explain:

- innate vs adaptive immunity
- antibodies
- T-cells
- inflammation

Modern relevance:

- immunotherapy
- vaccines
- autoimmune disease

Avoid excessive details.

---

# Lecture 3 — Genetics, Proteins & Biological Regulation

## Theme:

**Biology is programmable, but probabilistic and regulated.**

---

## Learning Goals

Students should understand:

- how genetic information flows
- what genes actually are
- why regulation matters more than “DNA code”
- mutation and evolution basics
- why modern biotech is possible

---

# Part 1 — DNA and Genes (15 min)

Explain:

- DNA structure
- genes
- chromosomes
- genome

Critical correction:

> Genes are not deterministic “blueprints.”

They are context-dependent instructions.

---

# Part 2 — Central Dogma (15 min)

Core flow:

DNA → RNA → Protein

Explain:

- transcription
- translation
- ribosomes
- codons

This is essential.

---

# Part 3 — Gene Regulation (15 min)

Arguably more important than genes themselves.

Teach:

- gene expression
- transcription factors
- epigenetics
- cell differentiation

Key insight:

> Different cell types mostly share the same DNA but express different programs.

Connect to:

- development
- cancer
- AI analogy of dynamic activation

---

# Part 4 — Mutation, Evolution & Selection (10 min)

Students need evolutionary thinking.

Explain:

- mutation
- selection
- drift
- adaptation

Connection:

- antibiotic resistance
- cancer evolution
- viral evolution

---

# Part 5 — Modern Biotechnology (5 min)

Very compressed overview:

- CRISPR
- sequencing
- mRNA
- synthetic biology
- gene therapy

Goal:
students should understand why biotech is advancing rapidly.

---

# Lecture 4 — Physiology, Disease & Pharmacology

## Theme:

**Disease is disrupted regulation across scales.**

---

## Learning Goals

Students should understand:

- what disease mechanistically means
- how organ systems coordinate
- how drugs intervene
- why side effects occur
- why medicine is probabilistic

---

# Part 1 — Physiology as Multi-Scale Coordination (15 min)

Introduce systems view:

- cells
- tissues
- organs
- organism

Briefly cover:

- nervous system
- cardiovascular system
- endocrine system

Focus on interaction rather than memorization.

---

# Part 2 — What Is Disease? (15 min)

Teach disease categories:

- infection
- cancer
- metabolic disease
- autoimmune disease
- neurodegeneration
- genetic disease

Key idea:

> Disease often emerges from network failures rather than single causes.

---

# Part 3 — Pharmacology Fundamentals (20 min)

Very important.

Teach:

- receptors
- agonists/antagonists
- dose-response
- therapeutic window
- pharmacokinetics vs pharmacodynamics

Explain:

- absorption
- metabolism
- toxicity

Critical insight:

> Drugs perturb systems; they rarely “fix” isolated components.

---

# Part 4 — Personalized Medicine & Biomarkers (10 min)

Explain:

- biomarkers
- diagnostics
- stratification
- genomics
- precision medicine

Connection to AI:

- multimodal data
- prediction
- patient clustering

---

# Lecture 5 — Clinical Trials, Evidence & Modern Biomedical Research

## Theme:

**Medicine advances through controlled uncertainty reduction.**

---

## Learning Goals

Students should understand:

- why trials exist
- how evidence is generated
- why medical evidence is hard
- common failure modes
- how AI intersects with medicine

---

# Part 1 — Why Clinical Trials Exist (10 min)

Key problem:

Humans are noisy systems.

Explain:

- placebo effects
- confounding
- bias
- regression to mean

---

# Part 2 — Trial Design (20 min)

Essential concepts:

- randomization
- control groups
- blinding
- endpoints
- inclusion/exclusion criteria

Explain phases:

- Phase I
- Phase II
- Phase III
- Phase IV

Students should understand:

- safety vs efficacy
- internal vs external validity

---

# Part 3 — Statistics in Medicine (10 min)

Assume students know statistics mathematically.

Focus instead on biomedical interpretation:

- effect size
- survival analysis intuition
- hazard ratios
- sensitivity/specificity
- ROC curves
- false positives

Critical concept:

> Statistical significance ≠ clinical significance.

---

# Part 4 — Translational Research & Drug Development (10 min)

Explain pipeline:

discovery → preclinical → trials → approval

Discuss:

- why drug development is expensive
- failure rates
- reproducibility crisis

---

# Part 5 — AI in Modern Biomedicine (10 min)

Conclude with frontier topics:

- foundation models for biology
- protein models
- multimodal medicine
- digital pathology
- clinical agents
- simulation / digital twins
- synthetic biology

But emphasize:

> The bottleneck is often not models, but reliable biological understanding, measurement, workflows, regulation, and evidence generation.

---

# What Students Should Know Afterwards

After 5 hours, students should be able to:

- read biomedical articles at a high level
- understand conversations with clinicians/researchers
- reason mechanistically about disease and treatment
- understand clinical trial discussions
- understand the role of AI in biology/medicine
- know what they do NOT know
