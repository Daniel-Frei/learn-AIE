# Crash Course in Medicine

## Theme: Medicine is the science and practice of diagnosing, treating, preventing, and managing human disease under uncertainty.

---

# Overall Learning Goals

By the end of the course, students should be able to:

1. Understand how doctors think about disease, symptoms, diagnosis, treatment, and prognosis.
2. Explain the difference between signs, symptoms, syndromes, diseases, and diagnoses.
3. Understand the major organ systems and the common disease categories affecting them.
4. Follow the basic logic of clinical reasoning and medical decision-making.
5. Understand the structure of healthcare delivery: outpatient care, emergency care, inpatient care, surgery, ICU, rehabilitation, and chronic care.
6. Understand the role of diagnostics: laboratory tests, imaging, pathology, biomarkers, and screening.
7. Understand the main treatment modalities: drugs, surgery, devices, behavioral interventions, rehabilitation, prevention, and palliative care.
8. Understand evidence-based medicine, clinical guidelines, clinical trials, risk-benefit reasoning, and medical uncertainty.
9. Speak more fluently with physicians, clinical researchers, hospital managers, and healthtech stakeholders.

# Lecture 1 — What Medicine Is: Disease, Patients, and Clinical Reasoning

## Theme

**Medicine is applied biology under uncertainty, in real patients, with real-world constraints.**

## Learning Goals

Students should understand:

- what medicine is and what makes it different from biology
- the difference between illness, disease, symptoms, signs, syndromes, and diagnoses
- how physicians move from a patient story to a differential diagnosis
- why medicine is probabilistic rather than purely deterministic
- the basic structure of a clinical encounter

## 1. Medicine vs Biology vs Healthcare

Medicine is not simply “human biology.” Biology explains how organisms work. Medicine asks: **what is wrong with this person, how serious is it, what can we do, and what should we do next?**

Important distinctions:

| Concept           | Meaning                                                     |
| ----------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------- |
| Biology           | How living systems work                                     |
| Pathophysiology   | How biological systems go wrong                             |
| Medicine          | Diagnosis, treatment, prevention, and management of disease |
| Healthcare        | The system that delivers medical care                       |
| Public health     | Health at the population level                              |
| Clinical research | How we generate evidence about interventions                |

A doctor must combine biological knowledge, patient communication, diagnostic reasoning, risk assessment, ethics, and practical constraints.

## 2. The Basic Medical Vocabulary

Students should learn these foundational distinctions:

| Term        | Meaning                                            | Example                                |
| ----------- | -------------------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------- |
| Symptom     | What the patient experiences                       | Chest pain, fatigue, nausea            |
| Sign        | What can be observed/measured                      | Fever, rash, low oxygen saturation     |
| Syndrome    | A recognizable cluster of findings                 | Metabolic syndrome, sepsis syndrome    |
| Disease     | A defined pathological process                     | Pneumonia, diabetes, cancer            |
| Diagnosis   | The clinician’s conclusion about what is happening | “Community-acquired pneumonia”         |
| Prognosis   | Expected course/outcome                            | “Good with antibiotics” or “high risk” |
| Comorbidity | Additional disease in same patient                 | Diabetes plus kidney disease           |
| Acute       | Short-term, rapid onset                            | Heart attack, appendicitis             |
| Chronic     | Long-term condition                                | Hypertension, COPD, diabetes           |

## 3. The Clinical Encounter

A typical medical encounter follows a rough structure:

1. **Chief complaint** — why the patient came.
2. **History of present illness** — the story of the current problem.
3. **Past medical history** — previous diseases, surgeries, hospitalizations.
4. **Medications and allergies**
5. **Family and social history**
6. **Review of systems**
7. **Physical examination**
8. **Initial assessment**
9. **Differential diagnosis**
10. **Diagnostic plan**
11. **Treatment plan**
12. **Follow-up / monitoring**

The key point: doctors rarely start with certainty. They start with a **problem representation**.

Example:

> “A 67-year-old man with diabetes and smoking history presents with sudden crushing chest pain radiating to the left arm.”

This immediately raises concern for myocardial infarction, but other possibilities remain.

## 4. Clinical Reasoning

Doctors reason through:

- pattern recognition
- probability
- anatomy
- physiology
- risk factors
- time course
- severity
- test results
- treatment response

The central tool is the **differential diagnosis**: a ranked list of possible explanations.

Example: chest pain.

Possible causes include:

| Category         | Example                                     |
| ---------------- | ------------------------------------------- |
| Cardiac          | Myocardial infarction, angina, pericarditis |
| Pulmonary        | Pulmonary embolism, pneumonia, pneumothorax |
| Gastrointestinal | Reflux, esophageal spasm                    |
| Musculoskeletal  | Rib injury, muscle strain                   |
| Psychological    | Panic attack                                |

A good clinician asks: **What is most likely? What is most dangerous? What must not be missed?**

## 5. Disease as Disrupted Function

Most diseases can be understood as disruptions of:

- structure
- function
- regulation
- defense
- perfusion
- growth
- metabolism
- communication

Examples:

| Disruption             | Example                             |
| ---------------------- | ----------------------------------- |
| Too little blood flow  | Stroke, heart attack                |
| Too much growth        | Cancer                              |
| Immune misfire         | Autoimmune disease                  |
| Infection              | Pneumonia, sepsis                   |
| Hormonal dysregulation | Diabetes, thyroid disease           |
| Mechanical failure     | Osteoarthritis, heart valve disease |
| Genetic defect         | Cystic fibrosis                     |
| Degeneration           | Alzheimer’s disease                 |

## 6. Key Takeaway

Medicine is about moving from **messy human presentation** to a **working diagnosis and management plan**, while balancing uncertainty, urgency, evidence, and patient values.

# Lecture 2 — The Human Body in Medicine: Organ Systems and Major Disease Categories

## Theme

**Most clinical medicine is organized by organ systems and recurring disease mechanisms.**

## Learning Goals

Students should understand:

- the major organ systems used in clinical medicine
- common disease categories across systems
- how pathophysiology connects symptoms to disease
- why specialists are organized around body systems
- the difference between local disease and systemic disease

## 1. The Organ-System Map of Medicine

Medicine is often organized by organ system:

| System               | Specialty                | Examples                                  |
| -------------------- | ------------------------ | ----------------------------------------- |
| Heart and vessels    | Cardiology               | Heart attack, heart failure, arrhythmia   |
| Lungs                | Pulmonology              | Asthma, COPD, pneumonia                   |
| Brain and nerves     | Neurology                | Stroke, epilepsy, Parkinson’s             |
| Digestive system     | Gastroenterology         | IBD, liver disease, ulcers                |
| Kidneys              | Nephrology               | Kidney failure, electrolyte disorders     |
| Hormones/metabolism  | Endocrinology            | Diabetes, thyroid disease                 |
| Blood                | Hematology               | Anemia, leukemia, clotting disorders      |
| Immune system        | Rheumatology/Immunology  | Lupus, rheumatoid arthritis               |
| Cancer               | Oncology                 | Solid tumors, chemotherapy, immunotherapy |
| Bones/joints/muscles | Orthopedics/Rheumatology | Fractures, arthritis                      |
| Skin                 | Dermatology              | Eczema, melanoma                          |
| Reproductive system  | Gynecology/Urology       | Pregnancy, infertility, prostate disease  |
| Mental health        | Psychiatry               | Depression, psychosis, anxiety            |

## 2. Major Disease Mechanisms

Instead of memorizing thousands of diseases, students should learn recurring mechanisms.

| Mechanism              | Examples                                        |
| ---------------------- | ----------------------------------------------- |
| Infection              | Pneumonia, meningitis, urinary tract infection  |
| Inflammation           | Asthma, inflammatory bowel disease              |
| Autoimmunity           | Multiple sclerosis, lupus, rheumatoid arthritis |
| Ischemia               | Stroke, myocardial infarction                   |
| Degeneration           | Alzheimer’s, osteoarthritis                     |
| Neoplasia              | Cancer                                          |
| Trauma                 | Fractures, internal bleeding                    |
| Metabolic dysfunction  | Diabetes, gout                                  |
| Genetic disease        | Sickle cell disease, cystic fibrosis            |
| Toxic injury           | Alcoholic liver disease, drug overdose          |
| Hormonal imbalance     | Hyperthyroidism, adrenal insufficiency          |
| Psychiatric/behavioral | Depression, addiction                           |

This gives students a framework for understanding almost any medical topic.

## 3. Cardiovascular Medicine as a Model

The cardiovascular system is central because many life-threatening diseases involve blood flow.

Core concepts:

- blood pressure
- cardiac output
- oxygen delivery
- arteries and veins
- atherosclerosis
- thrombosis
- heart failure
- arrhythmia

Example: myocardial infarction.

Basic mechanism:

1. Atherosclerotic plaque forms in coronary artery.
2. Plaque ruptures.
3. Blood clot forms.
4. Blood flow is blocked.
5. Heart muscle becomes ischemic.
6. Tissue dies if blood flow is not restored.

Clinical presentation:

- chest pain
- sweating
- nausea
- shortness of breath
- ECG changes
- elevated troponin

Treatment:

- antiplatelet therapy
- anticoagulation
- coronary angiography
- stent placement
- risk-factor management

## 4. Infection and Immunity

Students should understand:

- pathogens: bacteria, viruses, fungi, parasites
- host immune response
- local vs systemic infection
- antibiotics vs antivirals
- antimicrobial resistance
- vaccination
- sepsis

Important distinction:

A patient may be harmed both by the pathogen and by the body’s inflammatory response.

Example: sepsis is not just “infection”; it is life-threatening organ dysfunction caused by a dysregulated response to infection.

## 5. Cancer

Cancer is uncontrolled cellular growth with potential invasion and metastasis.

Core concepts:

- benign vs malignant
- tumor stage
- tumor grade
- metastasis
- biomarkers
- surgery
- chemotherapy
- radiotherapy
- targeted therapy
- immunotherapy
- screening

Important distinction:

| Concept    | Meaning                                     |
| ---------- | ------------------------------------------- |
| Stage      | How far cancer has spread                   |
| Grade      | How abnormal/aggressive cells look          |
| Biomarker  | Biological feature that may guide treatment |
| Metastasis | Spread to distant organs                    |

## 6. Chronic Disease

A large part of modern medicine is chronic disease management.

Examples:

- hypertension
- diabetes
- chronic kidney disease
- chronic obstructive pulmonary disease
- heart failure
- depression
- dementia
- osteoarthritis

Chronic care is not one dramatic cure. It is monitoring, risk reduction, medication adjustment, lifestyle support, and prevention of complications.

## 7. Key Takeaway

Most diseases can be understood by combining:

1. the affected organ system
2. the underlying mechanism
3. the time course
4. the severity
5. the patient context

# Lecture 3 — Diagnosis: Symptoms, Tests, Imaging, Screening, and Uncertainty

## Theme

**Diagnosis is probabilistic reasoning supported by clinical examination, tests, and imaging.**

## Learning Goals

Students should understand:

- how diagnostic reasoning works
- why tests do not simply give “truth”
- sensitivity, specificity, false positives, false negatives, and pre-test probability
- how laboratory tests, imaging, pathology, and biomarkers are used
- the difference between diagnosis and screening
- why overdiagnosis and incidental findings matter

## 1. From Symptom to Diagnosis

A symptom is not a diagnosis.

Example: shortness of breath can come from:

- asthma
- pneumonia
- heart failure
- pulmonary embolism
- anemia
- panic attack
- metabolic acidosis
- deconditioning

Doctors narrow the possibilities using:

- time course
- risk factors
- associated symptoms
- physical examination
- vital signs
- laboratory tests
- imaging
- response to treatment

## 2. Vital Signs

Vital signs are among the most important data in medicine.

| Vital Sign        | Why It Matters                   |
| ----------------- | -------------------------------- |
| Temperature       | Infection/inflammation           |
| Heart rate        | Stress, shock, arrhythmia, fever |
| Blood pressure    | Perfusion, cardiovascular risk   |
| Respiratory rate  | Early marker of deterioration    |
| Oxygen saturation | Gas exchange                     |
| Pain score        | Symptom severity, monitoring     |

A patient’s story matters, but abnormal vital signs often determine urgency.

## 3. Diagnostic Tests Are Probabilistic

Students with statistics backgrounds should understand this deeply.

Key concepts:

| Term                      | Meaning                                                       |
| ------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Sensitivity               | Probability test is positive if disease is present            |
| Specificity               | Probability test is negative if disease is absent             |
| False positive            | Test suggests disease, but disease is absent                  |
| False negative            | Test misses disease                                           |
| Positive predictive value | Probability patient has disease if test is positive           |
| Negative predictive value | Probability patient does not have disease if test is negative |
| Pre-test probability      | Estimated probability before testing                          |
| Post-test probability     | Probability after test result                                 |

The same test result means different things in different patients.

Example:

A positive test for a rare disease in a low-risk person may be more likely to be false positive than true positive.

## 4. Laboratory Medicine

Common types of lab tests:

| Test Type            | Examples                           | What It Tells You                  |
| -------------------- | ---------------------------------- | ---------------------------------- |
| Blood count          | Hemoglobin, white cells, platelets | Anemia, infection, clotting risk   |
| Chemistry panel      | Sodium, potassium, creatinine      | Kidney function, electrolytes      |
| Liver tests          | ALT, AST, bilirubin                | Liver injury/cholestasis           |
| Inflammatory markers | CRP, ESR                           | Inflammation/infection             |
| Coagulation tests    | INR, aPTT                          | Bleeding/clotting risk             |
| Cardiac biomarkers   | Troponin                           | Heart muscle injury                |
| Endocrine tests      | TSH, HbA1c                         | Thyroid, diabetes                  |
| Microbiology         | Cultures, PCR                      | Pathogen identification            |
| Genetics             | Sequencing, panels                 | Inherited risk/molecular diagnosis |

## 5. Imaging

Common imaging modalities:

| Modality   | Strengths                         | Typical Uses                              |
| ---------- | --------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------- |
| X-ray      | Fast, cheap, good for bones/lungs | Fracture, pneumonia                       |
| Ultrasound | No radiation, bedside use         | Pregnancy, gallbladder, heart, vessels    |
| CT         | Fast, detailed anatomy            | Trauma, stroke, cancer staging            |
| MRI        | Excellent soft tissue detail      | Brain, joints, spine                      |
| PET        | Metabolic activity                | Cancer staging, some neurology/cardiology |
| Endoscopy  | Direct visualization              | GI tract, biopsy                          |

Imaging often answers anatomical questions: **Where is the problem? What structure is affected?**

## 6. Pathology and Biopsy

Pathology is central in many diagnoses, especially cancer.

A biopsy can determine:

- whether tissue is benign or malignant
- tumor type
- grade
- margins
- inflammatory pattern
- molecular markers
- treatment-relevant features

In oncology, the pathologist’s report may determine the diagnosis, stage, and treatment plan.

## 7. Screening vs Diagnosis

Diagnosis investigates a symptomatic or high-suspicion problem.

Screening tests apparently healthy people to detect disease earlier.

Examples:

- mammography for breast cancer
- colonoscopy or stool testing for colorectal cancer
- Pap smear/HPV testing for cervical cancer
- blood pressure screening
- newborn screening

Screening can help, but it also creates risks:

- false positives
- anxiety
- unnecessary procedures
- overdiagnosis
- overtreatment

## 8. Key Takeaway

Tests do not replace clinical reasoning. They update probabilities. Good medicine asks: **Will this test change management?**

# Lecture 4 — Treatment and Care Pathways: Drugs, Surgery, Hospitals, and Chronic Care

## Theme

**Treatment is not just choosing an intervention; it is managing a patient through a care pathway.**

## Learning Goals

Students should understand:

- major categories of treatment
- how drugs, surgery, devices, procedures, rehabilitation, and behavioral interventions differ
- how care is organized across outpatient, inpatient, emergency, ICU, and long-term settings
- why chronic disease management dominates healthcare
- how doctors balance benefit, harm, patient preferences, and system constraints

## 1. Treatment Modalities

Major treatment categories:

| Treatment Type       | Examples                                    |
| -------------------- | ------------------------------------------- |
| Pharmacological      | Antibiotics, antihypertensives, insulin     |
| Surgical             | Appendectomy, tumor removal, bypass surgery |
| Procedural           | Endoscopy, catheter ablation, stenting      |
| Device-based         | Pacemaker, insulin pump, prosthesis         |
| Radiation            | Cancer radiotherapy                         |
| Behavioral/lifestyle | Diet, exercise, smoking cessation           |
| Psychological        | CBT, psychiatric medication                 |
| Rehabilitation       | Physiotherapy, occupational therapy         |
| Preventive           | Vaccination, screening, risk reduction      |
| Palliative           | Symptom relief, quality of life             |

## 2. Pharmacology in Clinical Medicine

Students do not need to memorize drug classes, but should understand how doctors think about drugs.

Key concepts:

| Concept             | Meaning                           |
| ------------------- | --------------------------------- |
| Indication          | Why the drug is used              |
| Contraindication    | When it should not be used        |
| Mechanism of action | How it works biologically         |
| Dose                | How much is given                 |
| Route               | Oral, IV, inhaled, topical, etc.  |
| Pharmacokinetics    | What the body does to the drug    |
| Pharmacodynamics    | What the drug does to the body    |
| Side effect         | Unwanted effect                   |
| Drug interaction    | One drug affects another          |
| Adherence           | Whether patient actually takes it |

Example: antibiotics.

A doctor considers:

- likely pathogen
- infection site
- severity
- local resistance patterns
- kidney/liver function
- allergies
- oral vs IV route
- duration
- narrowing therapy once cultures return

## 3. Surgery and Procedures

Surgery is often about physically correcting anatomy or removing pathology.

Examples:

- removing an inflamed appendix
- repairing a fracture
- removing a tumor
- bypassing blocked arteries
- replacing a joint
- transplanting an organ

Key surgical concepts:

- indication
- operative risk
- anesthesia risk
- perioperative management
- complications
- recovery
- informed consent

Procedures blur the line between medicine and surgery, such as coronary stenting, endoscopy, catheter ablation, and biopsies.

## 4. Levels of Care

Healthcare is organized into settings.

| Setting                    | Purpose                                    |
| -------------------------- | ------------------------------------------ |
| Primary care               | First contact, prevention, chronic disease |
| Specialist outpatient care | Focused expert evaluation                  |
| Emergency department       | Acute triage and stabilization             |
| Inpatient ward             | Hospital treatment and monitoring          |
| Intensive care unit        | Organ support and critical illness         |
| Operating room             | Surgery/procedures                         |
| Rehabilitation             | Functional recovery                        |
| Long-term care             | Ongoing support, frailty, disability       |
| Palliative care            | Symptom relief and goals-of-care support   |

## 5. Acute Care Pathway Example: Stroke

A stroke pathway shows how modern medicine works as a system.

1. Patient develops sudden weakness/speech problem.
2. Emergency services activated.
3. Time of symptom onset is established.
4. CT/MRI distinguishes ischemic vs hemorrhagic stroke.
5. Eligible ischemic stroke patients may receive thrombolysis or thrombectomy.
6. Patient monitored for complications.
7. Secondary prevention starts: antiplatelet therapy, anticoagulation if needed, statin, blood pressure control.
8. Rehabilitation begins.
9. Long-term follow-up manages disability and recurrence risk.

The key lesson: outcome depends not only on medical knowledge, but on logistics, timing, teams, and systems.

## 6. Chronic Care Pathway Example: Diabetes

Diabetes care involves:

- diagnosis by blood glucose/HbA1c
- lifestyle management
- medication selection
- monitoring blood sugar
- cardiovascular risk reduction
- kidney monitoring
- eye exams
- foot exams
- patient education
- adherence support
- prevention of complications

Chronic disease management is iterative. The question is not “Which drug cures it?” but “How do we reduce risk over years?”

## 7. Palliative Care

Palliative care is often misunderstood. It is not simply “giving up.” It focuses on:

- symptom control
- quality of life
- patient goals
- communication
- decision-making
- end-of-life care when appropriate

It can be combined with disease-directed treatment, especially in serious illness.

## 8. Key Takeaway

Treatment is a pathway involving diagnosis, intervention, monitoring, side effects, adherence, follow-up, and patient goals.

# Lecture 5 — Evidence, Healthcare Systems, and the Future of Medicine

## Theme

**Medicine is built on evidence, delivered by systems, and increasingly shaped by data and technology.**

## Learning Goals

Students should understand:

- evidence-based medicine
- clinical guidelines
- randomized trials and observational studies
- risk-benefit reasoning
- healthcare system structure
- hospital management basics
- quality, safety, and cost
- modern trends: digital health, AI, precision medicine, real-world evidence

## 1. Evidence-Based Medicine

Evidence-based medicine combines:

1. best available research evidence
2. clinical expertise
3. patient values and preferences

Evidence is not simply “the paper says so.” Doctors ask:

- Was the study well designed?
- Is the population similar to my patient?
- Is the effect clinically meaningful?
- What are the harms?
- How certain is the evidence?
- Does this apply in this setting?

## 2. Evidence Hierarchy

A simplified hierarchy:

| Evidence Type                   | Strengths                           | Limitations                      |
| ------------------------------- | ----------------------------------- | -------------------------------- |
| Mechanistic reasoning           | Biologically plausible              | Often misleading alone           |
| Case report                     | Detects rare events                 | Low generalizability             |
| Observational study             | Real-world data, larger populations | Confounding                      |
| Randomized controlled trial     | Strong causal inference             | Expensive, selective populations |
| Meta-analysis/systematic review | Summarizes evidence                 | Depends on included studies      |
| Guidelines                      | Practical recommendations           | May lag behind evidence          |

## 3. Clinical Trials

Students should understand the basic phases:

| Phase     | Main Question                                     |
| --------- | ------------------------------------------------- |
| Phase I   | Is it safe? What dose?                            |
| Phase II  | Does it show biological/clinical activity?        |
| Phase III | Does it work compared with standard care/placebo? |
| Phase IV  | What happens after approval in real-world use?    |

Core trial concepts:

- randomization
- control group
- blinding
- endpoints
- inclusion/exclusion criteria
- adverse events
- statistical power
- intention-to-treat analysis
- clinical significance vs statistical significance

## 4. Guidelines and Standard of Care

Clinical guidelines translate evidence into practical recommendations.

They influence:

- diagnosis
- treatment selection
- reimbursement
- quality metrics
- hospital protocols
- medico-legal standards

But guidelines are not automatic rules. Clinicians adapt them to:

- patient comorbidities
- contraindications
- local resources
- patient preferences
- uncertainty in the evidence

## 5. Healthcare Delivery and Hospital Management

A hospital is not just a building full of doctors. It is a complex operating system.

Important functions:

| Function            | Examples                                            |
| ------------------- | --------------------------------------------------- |
| Clinical operations | wards, operating rooms, outpatient clinics          |
| Nursing             | monitoring, medication administration, patient care |
| Diagnostics         | labs, imaging, pathology                            |
| Pharmacy            | medication safety and supply                        |
| Administration      | scheduling, billing, compliance                     |
| Quality and safety  | infection control, adverse event reporting          |
| IT and data         | EHR, clinical systems, analytics                    |
| Research            | trials, registries, ethics submissions              |
| Finance             | budgeting, reimbursement, cost control              |
| Legal/ethics        | consent, privacy, liability                         |

Students should understand that many healthcare problems are workflow and system problems, not only medical knowledge problems.

## 6. Quality and Safety

Medicine can harm as well as heal.

Important safety concepts:

- medication errors
- hospital-acquired infections
- surgical complications
- diagnostic delay
- communication failures
- handoff errors
- overtesting
- overtreatment
- undertreatment

Quality improvement asks:

- What process failed?
- How can the system be redesigned?
- What metric should be tracked?
- How do we reduce variation and error?

## 7. Modern Trends

Important trends in medicine:

| Trend                 | Meaning                                          |
| --------------------- | ------------------------------------------------ |
| Precision medicine    | Treatment tailored to biomarkers/genetics        |
| Digital health        | Apps, wearables, remote monitoring               |
| AI in medicine        | Decision support, documentation, imaging, triage |
| Real-world evidence   | Learning from routine clinical data              |
| Value-based care      | Paying for outcomes rather than volume           |
| Patient-centered care | Shared decision-making and patient goals         |
| Preventive medicine   | Reducing disease before it happens               |
| Integrated care       | Coordinating across specialties/settings         |

## 8. AI and Medicine

AI may support:

- documentation
- triage
- clinical decision support
- imaging interpretation
- patient education
- risk prediction
- drug discovery
- clinical trial design
- hospital operations
- remote monitoring

But AI in medicine faces special constraints:

- safety
- liability
- explainability
- bias
- data quality
- privacy
- clinical workflow integration
- regulatory approval
- physician trust
- patient trust

In medicine, a technically impressive system is not enough. It must fit clinical reality.

## 9. Key Takeaway

Modern medicine is a combination of science, uncertainty management, patient care, organizational systems, evidence generation, ethics, and technology.

# Final Course Summary

The course should leave students with this mental model:

> Medicine starts with a patient problem, translates it into a clinical representation, generates a differential diagnosis, uses tests to update probabilities, chooses treatments based on evidence and patient context, delivers care through complex systems, and constantly balances benefit, harm, uncertainty, cost, and human values.

That is the core of medicine in five hours.
