# Lecture 1 — What Medicine Is: Disease, Patients, and Clinical Reasoning

**Duration:** 60 minutes
**Theme:** _Medicine is applied biology under uncertainty, in real patients, with real-world constraints._

---

# Learning Objectives

By the end of this lecture, students should be able to:

1. Explain what medicine is and how it differs from biology, biomedical science, public health, and healthcare management.
2. Distinguish between symptoms, signs, syndromes, diseases, diagnoses, prognosis, and comorbidities.
3. Understand the basic structure of a clinical encounter.
4. Explain what a differential diagnosis is and why it is central to medical reasoning.
5. Understand why medicine is probabilistic rather than purely deterministic.
6. Describe how physicians move from a messy patient story to a structured clinical problem.
7. Recognize the importance of urgency, risk, uncertainty, and patient context in medical decisions.
8. Understand why medicine is both scientific and practical: it combines evidence, judgment, systems, ethics, and communication.

# Opening Frame

Start the lecture with a simple contrast:

> Biology asks: **How does the body work?**
> Medicine asks: **What is wrong with this person, how serious is it, what should we do, and what happens next?**

This distinction is essential.

Students may know biology, chemistry, statistics, or machine learning, but medicine is different because it is applied to individual humans under time pressure, uncertainty, incomplete information, ethical constraints, and organizational limitations.

A patient is not a clean dataset. A patient is a person with symptoms, fears, preferences, comorbidities, social constraints, incomplete memory, and biological variability.

# Part 1 — What Medicine Is

**Time:** 8 minutes

## Core Idea

Medicine is the science and practice of diagnosing, treating, preventing, and managing disease in humans.

But more specifically:

> Medicine is applied biological reasoning under uncertainty, constrained by patient values, evidence, safety, cost, logistics, and time.

Medicine combines several domains:

| Domain          | Role in Medicine                             |
| --------------- | -------------------------------------------- |
| Anatomy         | Where things are in the body                 |
| Physiology      | How the body normally functions              |
| Pathophysiology | How normal function breaks down              |
| Pharmacology    | How drugs affect the body                    |
| Epidemiology    | How disease behaves in populations           |
| Statistics      | How evidence and uncertainty are handled     |
| Ethics          | What should be done for this patient         |
| Communication   | How clinicians understand and guide patients |
| Operations      | How care is delivered in real systems        |

## Medicine vs Related Fields

Students should understand the difference between several overlapping but distinct fields.

| Field              | Central Question                                       | Example                                                     |
| ------------------ | ------------------------------------------------------ | ----------------------------------------------------------- |
| Biology            | How do living systems work?                            | How do immune cells recognize pathogens?                    |
| Biomedical science | What biological mechanisms explain disease?            | How does a mutation cause cancer?                           |
| Medicine           | What is wrong with this patient and what should we do? | Does this patient need antibiotics, surgery, or monitoring? |
| Public health      | How do we improve health at the population level?      | Should we run a vaccination campaign?                       |
| Healthcare         | How is care delivered and financed?                    | How should a hospital organize emergency services?          |
| Clinical research  | How do we know whether an intervention works?          | Does this drug reduce mortality?                            |

## Key Distinction: Disease vs Patient Care

A disease can be described abstractly.

Example:

> Pneumonia is an infection of the lung tissue that causes inflammation of the air spaces.

But patient care is concrete.

Example:

> A 78-year-old woman with diabetes and kidney disease has fever, confusion, low oxygen saturation, and a chest X-ray showing pneumonia. Should she be treated at home, admitted to hospital, or sent to intensive care?

The biological disease is only one part of the clinical problem.

The clinician must also ask:

- How severe is it?
- Is the patient stable?
- What else could this be?
- What is the risk of deterioration?
- Which tests are needed now?
- Which treatment is urgent?
- What are the patient’s comorbidities?
- What are the patient’s goals and preferences?
- What can realistically be done in this care setting?

## Teaching Example

Ask students:

> Two patients both have pneumonia. One is a healthy 22-year-old. The other is an 84-year-old with heart failure, kidney disease, and confusion. Do they have the same disease?

Answer:

They may have the same diagnosis, but not the same clinical situation.

Medicine is not only about naming the disease. It is about understanding the patient’s total risk, likely trajectory, and management needs.

# Part 2 — Core Medical Vocabulary

**Time:** 10 minutes

## Core Idea

To speak with clinicians, students need to understand the basic language of medicine.

Medicine uses precise distinctions. These distinctions are not just terminology; they shape clinical reasoning.

---

## Symptom

A **symptom** is something the patient experiences subjectively.

Examples:

- pain
- nausea
- fatigue
- dizziness
- shortness of breath
- headache
- weakness
- anxiety
- palpitations

Symptoms are often the starting point of medicine.

A patient usually does not say:

> “I have pulmonary embolism.”

They say:

> “I am short of breath and my chest hurts.”

The clinician must translate symptoms into possible medical explanations.

---

## Sign

A **sign** is something externally observable or measurable.

Examples:

- fever
- high blood pressure
- low oxygen saturation
- rash
- swelling
- abnormal heart sound
- abnormal reflex
- blood in stool
- confusion observed by clinician
- abnormal ECG

The distinction:

| Symptom                  | Sign                              |
| ------------------------ | --------------------------------- |
| Patient reports it       | Clinician observes or measures it |
| Subjective               | More objective                    |
| “I feel short of breath” | Oxygen saturation is 86%          |
| “I feel feverish”        | Temperature is 39.2°C             |

Both matter.

Some dangerous diseases may initially have few signs. Some severe symptoms may occur without major objective abnormalities.

---

## Syndrome

A **syndrome** is a recognizable cluster of signs and symptoms that tend to occur together, but may have multiple causes.

Examples:

| Syndrome                 | Meaning                                                        |
| ------------------------ | -------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Sepsis syndrome          | Infection plus organ dysfunction                               |
| Metabolic syndrome       | Obesity, insulin resistance, hypertension, lipid abnormalities |
| Nephrotic syndrome       | Protein loss in urine, swelling, low blood albumin             |
| Acute coronary syndrome  | Reduced blood flow to heart muscle                             |
| Irritable bowel syndrome | Chronic abdominal symptoms without obvious structural disease  |

A syndrome is often a pattern before a final cause is known.

Example:

A patient can have “acute coronary syndrome” before clinicians know exactly which coronary artery is blocked or how severe the heart injury is.

---

## Disease

A **disease** is a pathological process affecting the body.

Examples:

- pneumonia
- diabetes mellitus
- myocardial infarction
- rheumatoid arthritis
- colon cancer
- asthma
- chronic kidney disease
- multiple sclerosis
- Alzheimer’s disease

A disease usually has some combination of:

- cause
- mechanism
- anatomical location
- typical presentation
- natural history
- complications
- treatments

But diseases are often heterogeneous. Two patients with the same disease label can be very different.

---

## Diagnosis

A **diagnosis** is the clinician’s conclusion about what condition best explains the patient’s presentation.

Examples:

- community-acquired pneumonia
- type 2 diabetes mellitus
- ischemic stroke
- major depressive disorder
- acute appendicitis
- stage II colon cancer

A diagnosis can be:

| Type                   | Example                                  |
| ---------------------- | ---------------------------------------- |
| Certain                | Biopsy-confirmed colon cancer            |
| Probable               | Likely viral infection                   |
| Working diagnosis      | Suspected pulmonary embolism             |
| Rule-out diagnosis     | Rule out myocardial infarction           |
| Differential diagnosis | Several possible causes still considered |

Important point:

> Diagnosis is often not a single moment. It is a process.

Doctors often work with provisional diagnoses and update them as new evidence arrives.

---

## Prognosis

A **prognosis** is the expected future course of a disease.

Questions of prognosis:

- Will the patient recover?
- How quickly?
- Is the disease curable?
- Is it likely to recur?
- What is the risk of death?
- What is the risk of disability?
- What complications are likely?
- What happens with and without treatment?

Examples:

| Disease       | Prognostic Question                               |
| ------------- | ------------------------------------------------- |
| Cancer        | What is the 5-year survival probability?          |
| Stroke        | Will the patient regain speech or mobility?       |
| Heart failure | What is the risk of hospitalization or death?     |
| Pneumonia     | Can the patient recover at home or need ICU care? |
| Dementia      | How quickly will cognitive function decline?      |

Prognosis is crucial in communication with patients and families.

---

## Comorbidity

A **comorbidity** is another disease or condition the patient has at the same time.

Example:

A patient with pneumonia may also have:

- diabetes
- kidney disease
- heart failure
- COPD
- dementia
- obesity
- depression

Comorbidities matter because they affect:

- risk
- treatment options
- drug dosing
- complications
- recovery
- prognosis
- clinical trial eligibility
- hospital resource use

A treatment that is safe in one patient may be risky in another because of comorbidities.

---

## Acute vs Chronic

| Term             | Meaning                               | Example                            |
| ---------------- | ------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------- |
| Acute            | Rapid onset, short-term, often urgent | Appendicitis, heart attack, stroke |
| Chronic          | Long-term, persistent                 | Diabetes, hypertension, COPD       |
| Acute-on-chronic | Sudden worsening of chronic disease   | Heart failure exacerbation         |

This distinction shapes care.

Acute medicine often asks:

> What must be done now?

Chronic medicine often asks:

> How do we reduce risk over months and years?

# Part 3 — The Patient Encounter

**Time:** 12 minutes

## Core Idea

The clinical encounter is the basic unit of medicine.

A doctor takes an unstructured human problem and turns it into a structured clinical representation.

---

## The Basic Structure

A typical medical encounter includes:

1. Chief complaint
2. History of present illness
3. Past medical history
4. Medications
5. Allergies
6. Family history
7. Social history
8. Review of systems
9. Physical examination
10. Initial assessment
11. Differential diagnosis
12. Diagnostic plan
13. Treatment plan
14. Follow-up plan

Students do not need to memorize every detail, but they should understand the logic.

---

## Chief Complaint

The **chief complaint** is the main reason the patient seeks care.

Examples:

- “Chest pain”
- “Shortness of breath”
- “Abdominal pain”
- “Fever”
- “Headache”
- “Fatigue”
- “Back pain”
- “Blood in stool”
- “Loss of consciousness”

The chief complaint is usually not the diagnosis. It is the starting point.

---

## History of Present Illness

The **history of present illness** is the detailed story of the current problem.

Clinicians ask:

- When did it start?
- Was the onset sudden or gradual?
- Where is the symptom located?
- How severe is it?
- What makes it better or worse?
- Is it constant or intermittent?
- Are there associated symptoms?
- Has this happened before?
- What was the patient doing when it began?
- Has anything changed recently?

Example: chest pain.

Important questions:

- Is it pressure-like, sharp, burning, tearing?
- Does it radiate to the arm, jaw, back?
- Did it start during exertion?
- Is there shortness of breath?
- Is there sweating or nausea?
- Is there fever or cough?
- Is there leg swelling?
- Is there a history of heart disease?

The same symptom can mean very different things depending on context.

---

## Past Medical History

Past medical history includes known diseases and major previous medical events.

Examples:

- diabetes
- hypertension
- heart attack
- stroke
- cancer
- kidney disease
- asthma
- surgeries
- hospitalizations
- psychiatric history
- pregnancy history where relevant

Past history changes the meaning of current symptoms.

Example:

Chest pain in a 25-year-old with no risk factors is different from chest pain in a 72-year-old with diabetes and prior coronary artery disease.

---

## Medications

Medication history matters because drugs can:

- treat current disease
- cause symptoms
- interact with new treatments
- indicate underlying diagnoses
- affect test results
- increase bleeding or infection risk

Examples:

| Medication Type        | Why It Matters                 |
| ---------------------- | ------------------------------ |
| Blood thinners         | Bleeding risk                  |
| Insulin                | Diabetes; risk of hypoglycemia |
| Steroids               | Immune suppression             |
| Chemotherapy           | Cancer; infection risk         |
| Antihypertensives      | Blood pressure effects         |
| Psychiatric medication | Side effects and interactions  |

A medication list often tells a story about the patient’s medical background.

---

## Allergies

Allergies matter especially for drug selection.

Example:

If a patient has a severe penicillin allergy, antibiotic choices may change.

But clinicians must also distinguish:

- true allergy
- side effect
- intolerance
- unknown reaction

Many patients report “allergy” when they mean nausea, fatigue, or mild rash. This can affect treatment choices.

---

## Family History

Family history is relevant for inherited or familial risk.

Examples:

- early heart disease
- breast or colon cancer
- genetic disorders
- autoimmune disease
- psychiatric illness
- sudden cardiac death

Family history changes pre-test probability.

---

## Social History

Social history is often medically crucial.

It includes:

- smoking
- alcohol use
- drug use
- occupation
- housing
- family support
- diet
- exercise
- travel
- sexual history where relevant
- financial constraints
- ability to follow treatment

Example:

A patient may be prescribed an ideal medication but not be able to afford it, store it, understand it, or take it reliably.

Medicine fails if it ignores social reality.

---

## Physical Examination

Physical examination is structured observation.

It includes:

- general appearance
- vital signs
- heart exam
- lung exam
- abdominal exam
- neurological exam
- skin exam
- musculoskeletal exam
- focused examination based on complaint

A key concept:

> Physical examination is not random checking. It is hypothesis-guided.

If the patient has chest pain, the doctor looks for clues suggesting heart disease, lung disease, vascular disease, abdominal disease, or musculoskeletal disease.

---

## Vital Signs

Vital signs are among the highest-value data in clinical medicine.

| Vital Sign        | Why It Matters                         |
| ----------------- | -------------------------------------- |
| Temperature       | Infection, inflammation                |
| Heart rate        | Stress, shock, arrhythmia, pain, fever |
| Blood pressure    | Perfusion, cardiovascular risk, shock  |
| Respiratory rate  | Early marker of deterioration          |
| Oxygen saturation | Gas exchange                           |
| Pain score        | Symptom severity and monitoring        |

Important teaching point:

> Respiratory rate is often one of the most important but underappreciated vital signs.

A patient who “looks okay” but has abnormal vital signs may be seriously ill.

# Part 4 — Clinical Reasoning and Differential Diagnosis

**Time:** 15 minutes

## Core Idea

Clinical reasoning is the process of turning patient information into a ranked set of possible explanations and action steps.

The central concept is the **differential diagnosis**.

---

## What Is a Differential Diagnosis?

A **differential diagnosis** is a list of possible diagnoses that could explain the patient’s presentation.

It is not just a random list. It is usually ranked by:

1. probability
2. danger
3. treatability
4. time sensitivity
5. fit with the patient’s presentation

Example: chest pain.

Possible causes:

| Category         | Possible Diagnosis                            |
| ---------------- | --------------------------------------------- |
| Cardiac          | Heart attack, angina, pericarditis            |
| Pulmonary        | Pulmonary embolism, pneumonia, pneumothorax   |
| Gastrointestinal | Reflux, esophageal spasm, gallbladder disease |
| Musculoskeletal  | Rib injury, muscle strain                     |
| Vascular         | Aortic dissection                             |
| Psychiatric      | Panic attack                                  |

The clinician asks:

> What is most likely?
> What is most dangerous?
> What must not be missed?

---

## The “Most Likely” vs “Most Dangerous” Distinction

This is one of the most important ideas in medicine.

Example:

A young person with chest pain may most likely have reflux, anxiety, or muscle pain.

But the doctor must still consider dangerous causes:

- myocardial infarction
- pulmonary embolism
- pneumothorax
- aortic dissection

A diagnosis can be unlikely but important because missing it would be catastrophic.

This is different from many ordinary reasoning tasks, where we mostly care about the most probable explanation.

Medicine cares about the combination of:

- probability
- severity
- reversibility
- urgency

---

## Pattern Recognition

Experienced doctors often use pattern recognition.

Example:

> Crushing central chest pain radiating to the left arm, with sweating and nausea, in an older patient with diabetes and smoking history.

This pattern suggests myocardial infarction.

Pattern recognition is fast and useful, but it can be biased.

Risks:

- premature closure
- confirmation bias
- availability bias
- anchoring
- ignoring atypical presentations

---

## Analytical Reasoning

Analytical reasoning is slower and more explicit.

It asks:

- What are the possible causes?
- Which findings support each one?
- Which findings argue against it?
- What test would discriminate between them?
- What diagnosis would be dangerous to miss?
- What treatment is safe before certainty?

Analytical reasoning is especially important when:

- the case is unusual
- the patient has multiple comorbidities
- symptoms are vague
- initial tests conflict
- treatment has major risks
- the patient is deteriorating

---

## Problem Representation

A **problem representation** is a compact clinical summary of the patient.

Bad version:

> “Patient has chest pain.”

Better version:

> “A 67-year-old man with diabetes, hypertension, and smoking history presents with sudden-onset central chest pressure radiating to the left arm, associated with sweating and shortness of breath.”

This representation includes:

- age
- risk factors
- key symptom
- timing
- character
- associated symptoms
- relevant negatives if needed

Problem representations help clinicians communicate efficiently.

---

## Illness Scripts

Doctors often think in “illness scripts.”

An illness script is a mental model of a disease, including:

- typical patient
- risk factors
- pathophysiology
- symptoms
- signs
- test findings
- disease course
- treatment

Example: myocardial infarction illness script.

| Element   | Typical Pattern                             |
| --------- | ------------------------------------------- |
| Patient   | Older, cardiovascular risk factors          |
| Mechanism | Coronary artery blockage                    |
| Symptoms  | Chest pressure, radiation, sweating, nausea |
| Signs     | May be normal or unstable                   |
| Tests     | ECG changes, elevated troponin              |
| Treatment | Antiplatelets, anticoagulation, reperfusion |

Students do not need to memorize many scripts yet. They should understand that doctors compare the patient’s presentation to known disease patterns.

---

## Case Example: Chest Pain

Use the following case throughout the section.

### Case

> A 62-year-old man comes to the emergency department with chest pain. The pain started 45 minutes ago while walking uphill. He describes it as pressure in the center of his chest. It radiates to his left arm. He feels nauseous and sweaty. He has hypertension, type 2 diabetes, and smoked for 30 years.

Ask students:

1. What are the key symptoms?
2. What are the key risk factors?
3. What diagnoses are possible?
4. Which diagnosis is most concerning?
5. What tests would be useful?
6. What treatment might be urgent?

### Discussion

Important features:

- age
- male sex
- exertional onset
- pressure-like pain
- radiation to left arm
- nausea and sweating
- diabetes
- smoking
- hypertension

Most concerning diagnosis:

> Acute coronary syndrome / myocardial infarction.

But differential still includes:

- pulmonary embolism
- aortic dissection
- reflux
- pneumonia
- musculoskeletal pain
- panic attack

Initial tests may include:

- ECG
- troponin
- vital signs
- oxygen saturation
- blood tests
- chest X-ray depending on presentation

Initial management may include:

- monitoring
- aspirin
- nitroglycerin in selected cases
- anticoagulation depending on diagnosis
- urgent cardiology involvement if ECG shows major myocardial infarction pattern

The point is not to train students to treat a heart attack. The point is to show how doctors move from story to structured reasoning.

# Part 5 — Uncertainty, Risk, and Decision-Making

**Time:** 10 minutes

## Core Idea

Medicine is full of uncertainty. The goal is not always certainty; the goal is good decision-making under uncertainty.

---

## Why Medicine Is Probabilistic

Medicine is probabilistic because:

- symptoms are nonspecific
- diseases overlap
- patients vary biologically
- tests are imperfect
- history can be incomplete
- presentations can be atypical
- comorbidities interact
- treatments have uncertain effects
- evidence comes from populations, but decisions concern individuals

Example:

Shortness of breath could be caused by:

- pneumonia
- asthma
- heart failure
- pulmonary embolism
- anemia
- panic attack
- metabolic acidosis
- deconditioning

No single symptom gives certainty.

---

## Pre-Test and Post-Test Probability

Students with statistics knowledge should understand medicine as probability updating.

Before a test, the clinician has a **pre-test probability**.

After the test, the probability changes.

Example:

A positive cardiac troponin test means something different in:

1. a 70-year-old with crushing chest pain
2. a 25-year-old marathon runner with no chest pain
3. a patient with severe kidney disease
4. a critically ill patient with sepsis

The test result is interpreted in context.

The same data point does not have the same meaning in every patient.

---

## Thresholds for Action

Doctors often act before certainty.

There are practical thresholds:

| Threshold           | Meaning                                                  |
| ------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------- |
| Testing threshold   | Probability high enough to justify testing               |
| Treatment threshold | Probability high enough to justify treatment             |
| Admission threshold | Risk high enough to justify hospital care                |
| ICU threshold       | Risk high enough to require intensive monitoring/support |
| No-action threshold | Probability low enough that observation is reasonable    |

Example:

If pulmonary embolism is possible but unlikely, a blood test may be enough.

If pulmonary embolism is likely and dangerous, imaging and treatment may be urgent.

The decision depends on:

- probability of disease
- severity if missed
- risk of test
- risk of treatment
- patient stability
- available resources

---

## Benefit-Harm Reasoning

Every medical intervention can help and harm.

Examples:

| Intervention   | Potential Benefit         | Potential Harm                       |
| -------------- | ------------------------- | ------------------------------------ |
| Antibiotic     | Treat bacterial infection | Allergy, resistance, side effects    |
| CT scan        | Detect serious disease    | Radiation, incidental findings       |
| Surgery        | Remove/correct pathology  | Bleeding, infection, anesthesia risk |
| Blood thinner  | Prevent clot              | Bleeding                             |
| Screening test | Detect disease early      | False positives, overdiagnosis       |
| ICU care       | Organ support             | Delirium, invasive complications     |

Medicine is not “do everything possible.” It is choosing actions where expected benefit justifies expected harm.

---

## Patient Values

Clinical decisions also depend on patient values.

Examples:

- A patient may prioritize survival at all costs.
- Another may prioritize independence and quality of life.
- Another may refuse blood transfusion.
- Another may prefer less aggressive treatment.
- Another may accept high risk for a chance of cure.

Evidence tells us what tends to happen. It does not automatically tell us what the patient should choose.

This is especially important in:

- cancer treatment
- intensive care
- chronic disease
- surgery
- end-of-life care
- preventive medicine

# Part 6 — Summary Case and Key Takeaways

**Time:** 5 minutes

## Final Integrative Case

Present a short case and walk through the full logic.

### Case

> A 74-year-old woman with diabetes and chronic kidney disease comes to the hospital because she has been confused for one day. Her daughter says she has had a cough and poor appetite for three days. Her temperature is 38.7°C, heart rate 112, blood pressure 92/58, respiratory rate 26, and oxygen saturation 89%.

Ask students:

1. What are the symptoms?
2. What are the signs?
3. What are the comorbidities?
4. What diagnoses are possible?
5. What is dangerous here?
6. Why is this more than “just a cough”?
7. What kind of care setting might she need?

### Discussion

Symptoms:

- confusion
- cough
- poor appetite

Signs:

- fever
- fast heart rate
- low blood pressure
- fast respiratory rate
- low oxygen saturation

Comorbidities:

- diabetes
- chronic kidney disease

Possible diagnosis:

- pneumonia with sepsis
- viral respiratory infection
- heart failure
- pulmonary embolism
- urinary infection with delirium
- metabolic disturbance

Danger signals:

- confusion
- low blood pressure
- low oxygen
- fast breathing
- older age
- comorbidities

The key clinical idea:

> The same “infection” can be mild in one patient and life-threatening in another.

---

# Main Takeaways

Students should leave Lecture 1 with the following mental model:

1. Medicine starts with a patient problem, not a textbook disease.
2. Symptoms are subjective experiences; signs are observed or measured findings.
3. A diagnosis is a clinical conclusion, often reached gradually.
4. A differential diagnosis is a ranked list of possible explanations.
5. Doctors care about what is likely, but also what is dangerous.
6. Tests update probabilities; they do not simply reveal truth.
7. Treatment decisions depend on benefit, harm, urgency, patient values, and system constraints.
8. Medicine is both scientific and practical.
9. Clinical reasoning is structured uncertainty management.
10. The patient’s context can change everything.
